### 1) Core Market Logic
The physical execution of an LPG trade is a high-stakes logistical exercise 
requiring scientific precision and strict legal compliance [1, 2]. Because LPG 
must be maintained in a liquid state—either through heavy pressurization or 
extreme refrigeration (e.g., -42°C for propane)—the infrastructure tolerances 
are razor-thin [3, 4]. Any delay or technical failure during the physical 
lifecycle doesn't just delay the cargo; it actively degrades the margin through
daily demurrage penalties, bunker fuel consumption, or catastrophic terminal 
rejections [2, 4]. 

### 2) Key Concepts
*   **The Operations Lifecycle:** The chronological chain of events from the 
moment the Sales and Purchase Agreement (SPA) is signed to the moment the final
MT103 cash settlement hits our bank account [5].
*   **Vessel Vetting:** The strict safety screening process (using SIRE and 
OCIMF standards) a ship must pass before a terminal will allow it to berth [5].
*   **Notice of Readiness (NOR):** The formal legal trigger tendered by the 
ship's master confirming the vessel has arrived and is ready to load or 
discharge, starting the "laytime" clock [6].
*   **Statement of Facts (SOF):** The minute-by-minute operational log signed 
by all parties at the port, used as the ultimate evidence for calculating 
demurrage claims [7].

### 3) How Traders Actually Use This
Traders do not work in isolation; they are in constant communication with the 
Ops desk. A trader must understand this lifecycle to know when to "roll" a 
financial hedge if a physical vessel is delayed, or when to renegotiate pricing
terms if the cargo slips into a different calendar month [8, 9]. For Hengyi's 
export flows, we use this lifecycle to perfectly time our Brunei refinery tank 
drawdowns, ensuring we never hit "tank-tops" (full storage) while maximizing 
our CFR China delivery premiums [10, 11].

### 4) Step-by-Step Trading Example: The Physical Lifecycle & Risks
Here is the detailed chronological lifecycle of a single LPG spot cargo, 
highlighting the specific financial risks your Ops team must manage at every 
single link.

#### Step 1: Nomination & Declarations
*   **The Process:** After the SPA is signed, the responsible party (the buyer 
in FOB, or the seller in CFR/CIF) officially "nominates" a specific VLGC and a 
loading window (laycan) [2, 5]. This nomination includes the vessel's details, 
ETA, previous three cargoes, and the required loading temperature [6, 12, 13]. 
The loading terminal must then accept the vessel after vetting its safety 
records [5].
*   **⚠️ KEY FINANCIAL RISKS:**
    *   **Vetting Rejection:** If the nominated vessel fails the terminal's 
SIRE inspection requirements, the ship is rejected [5]. The trader is left 
scrambling for a replacement vessel, risking massive late penalties or a 
canceled trade.
    *   **Tank-Top Shut-ins:** If the vessel nomination is delayed and the ship
misses its laycan at our Brunei refinery, our highly expensive cryogenic 
storage tanks will fill to capacity ("tank-tops") [10]. This forces the 
catastrophic and incredibly expensive decision to shut in upstream oil and gas 
production [10].

#### Step 2: Letter of Credit Issuance and Review (LC Issuance & Negotiation)
*   **The Process:** Before the seller confirms loading, the buyer's bank sends
an MT799 pre-advice, followed by opening an operative Standby Letter of Credit 
(SBLC) or standard LC [14]. The seller's bank meticulously reviews the LC to 
ensure it perfectly matches the agreed Incoterms and SPA [15].
*   **⚠️ KEY FINANCIAL RISKS:**
    *   **Documentary Discrepancies:** Banks will only release funds if the 
presentation documents exactly match the LC terms [16]. A simple typo on the 
Incoterm or Bill of Lading can result in non-payment or millions in frozen 
working capital.
    *   **LC Expiration:** If the physical loading of the vessel slips past the
specific loading period stated in the LC, the LC becomes invalid. The buyer 
must grant an extension, or the seller must suspend loading, causing the ship 
to sit idle and accrue demurrage [17].

#### Step 3: Vessel Chartering & Loading
*   **The Process:** The vessel arrives and tenders its NOR [6]. Before 
loading, independent inspectors (like SGS) conduct a pre-shipment survey [5]. 
The ship's tanks must be prepared—first "dried" (dew point < -40°C), then 
"inerted" (oxygen < 5%), and finally "gassed-up" and cooled down at a 
controlled rate (< 10°C/hour) [1, 18]. Loading commences and the Bill of Lading
(BL) is issued [5].
*   **⚠️ KEY FINANCIAL RISKS:**
    *   **Hydrate Blockages (Tank Prep Failure):** If the tanks are not 
properly dried, even 25 liters of water vapor can freeze during the cool-down 
phase, creating "hydrate" blockages in pumps and valves, delaying the loading 
by days [1].
    *   **Off-Spec Rejection:** If the independent inspector finds the cargo is
chemically "off-spec" (e.g., high sulfur or methanol limits), it will be 
disastrous for downstream buyers like PDH plants, potentially destroying the 
cargo's value [19]. 
    *   **Loading Demurrage:** If the terminal loads too slowly and exceeds the
allowed laytime (e.g., 24 to 36 hours), the seller incurs demurrage penalties 
that can easily exceed $50,000 per day (*illustrative rate*), eating directly 
into the trade margin [4, 20, 21].

#### Step 4: Voyage in Transit
*   **The Process:** The vessel departs. During the sea passage, the crew must 
carefully monitor and control the cargo's temperature, pressure, and liquid 
levels [22]. The ship's reliquefaction plant constantly cools the boil-off gas 
to maintain the cargo in its liquid state [4, 22]. 
*   **⚠️ KEY FINANCIAL RISKS:**
    *   **Reliquefaction Failure:** If the ship's cooling plant fails 
mid-voyage, the cargo will warm up and expand [4]. Discharge terminals will 
refuse to accept warm LPG, resulting in catastrophic delays and potential cargo
loss [4]. 
    *   **Freight & Routing Volatility:** Weather delays or geopolitical 
chokepoints (like Panama Canal drought restrictions or Hormuz war risks) can 
force a vessel to reroute via the Cape of Good Hope, adding 10 to 15 days to 
the voyage [23, 24]. This spikes bunker (fuel) costs and triggers late-delivery
penalties [23].

#### Step 5: Discharge & Settlement
*   **The Process:** The vessel arrives at the destination port (e.g., Ningbo, 
China), tenders NOR, and connects to the unloading arm [25, 26]. Following 
discharge, the final quantity is calculated [27]. The seller presents the 
complete set of shipping documents to the bank, and final payment is settled 
(typically via MT103 swift transfer) [5, 19]. 
*   **⚠️ KEY FINANCIAL RISKS:**
    *   **Volume Slippage:** Because LPG expands and contracts with 
temperature, the volume discharged rarely perfectly matches the volume loaded 
[8]. If the trader hedged a specific volume on paper, but the physical outturn 
is short, the trader is left holding unbacked ("naked") paper exposure [8].
    *   **Pricing Formula Rounding Errors:** Complex Asian LNG/LPG pricing 
formulas often require converting energy units (MMBtu) into volumetric/mass 
units (Metric Tons) for settlement [28]. Because every cargo has a slightly 
different energy density, rounding errors accumulate, leading to pricing 
discrepancies and margin erosion [28]. 
    *   **Discharge Demurrage:** Port congestion or terminal equipment failure 
can trap the vessel at the discharge port. Demurrage liability is a "liquidated
damage" that accrues minute by minute once laytime expires, heavily impacting 
the CFR seller's P&L [21].

### 5) Trader Decision Process
**Go:**
*   The nominated vessel passes Hengyi's SIRE vetting and has fully functioning
reliquefaction plants.
*   The Buyer's LC is operative, confirmed by our prime bank, and exactly 
mirrors our SPA terms [14].
*   The laycan aligns perfectly with Brunei's production schedule to avoid 
tank-tops [10].

**No-Go (Trade Killers):**
*   **Sanctions/Compliance Failure:** The vessel or counterparty fails KYC or 
is linked to sanctioned origins [29, 30].
*   **Uninsured Voyage:** War-risk insurance is pulled for a critical transit 
route, stranding the vessel [31].
*   **Mismatched Inspection Clauses:** The buyer demands outturn (discharge 
port) quantity settlement while our supplier only guarantees load port 
quantity, exposing the desk to transit loss risk.

### 6) Common Trader Mistakes
*   **Treating Ops as an Afterthought:** Booking a trade and forgetting about 
it. If a trader isn't tracking the vessel's daily ETA, they will fail to roll 
their financial hedges when the physical delivery slips into a new pricing 
month [8].
*   **Ignoring LC Expiry Dates:** Assuming a delayed vessel can just load a few
days late. If it loads past the LC date without an explicit amendment, you are 
shipping a $25 million cargo with zero bank guarantee protecting your payment 
[17].
