**Quantitative Freight Economics & Arb Window Modeling**

To execute the USGC-to-Asia arbitrage effectively, your pricing algorithms must
bypass static spreadsheet approximations and dynamically compile every 
micro-structural cost component. Moving 44,000 MT of refrigerated propane from 
a USGC FOB loading terminal to a CFR delivery in Chiba or Ningbo requires 
pricing the netback with precision, dynamically integrating real-time logistics
constraints via Kpler or Vortexa API feeds [1].

### I. The Exact USGC-Asia Arbitrage Netback Formula

Within your Openlink Endur or proprietary CTRM, the instantaneous Arbitrage 
Margin ($\Pi_{Arb}$) must be modeled as a continuous function of time ($t$), 
where the spread is defined as the CFR Far East Index (AFEI) minus the sum of 
the FOB Mont Belvieu (MB) acquisition cost and the Total Logistics Cost 
($\Sigma L$) [2].

The granular formula is:
$$\Pi_{Arb}(t) = AFEI(t_{del}) - \left[ MB(t_{load}) + \Phi_{Term} + 
\Psi_{Baltic} + \Gamma_{Panama} + \Omega_{Bunker} + \Delta_{Ins/Loss} \right]$$

**Mathematical Breakdown of Operations (Ops) Components:**

*   **$MB(t_{load})$ & $AFEI(t_{del})$**: The prompt/forward swap values for 
the loading window in Mont Belvieu (converted from cents per gallon to $/mt) 
and the delivery window in the Far East [3].
*   **$\Phi_{Term}$ (Terminal Lifting Fees)**: The contracted throughput fee at
USGC terminals (e.g., Enterprise, Targa, Nederland), typically modeled around 
$5.25$ cpg or ~$26-$27/mt [4, 5].
*   **$\Psi_{Baltic}$ (Base VLGC Freight)**: The spot Baltic route equivalent 
(Houston-to-Chiba) [2]. This serves as the baseline freight cost, assuming 
standard 14-knot laden speed and standard route. 
*   **$\Gamma_{Panama}$ (Panama Canal Premium)**: The excess cost above 
standard transit assumptions. It is calculated as:
    $$\Gamma_{Panama} = \frac{ (\kappa \times \$2.75) + \lambda_{Auction} + 
\rho_{FWS} }{ \text{Cargo Size (MT)} }$$
    Where $\kappa$ is the Neopanamax capacity tonnage in $m^3$ [6], 
$\lambda_{Auction}$ is the dynamic transit slot auction fee (which carries a 
base rate of $100,000 but scales aggressively during congestion) [7], and 
$\rho_{FWS}$ is the variable Fresh Water Surcharge linked to Gatun Lake draught
levels [8-10].
*   **$\Omega_{Bunker}$ (Bunker Consumption Adjustment)**: The differential 
bunker cost derived from vessel speed adjustments, wait times, or dual-fuel 
efficiencies. A standard VLGC burns ~65 mt/day laden [11]. If utilizing a 
dual-fuel ME-LGIP engine burning cargo boil-off, the consumption cost drops 
from the traditional VLSFO rate of $27,000-$35,000/day to $20,000-$24,000/day 
[2, 12], requiring an exact daily delta adjustment in the deal economics.
*   **$\Delta_{Ins/Loss}$ (Insurance & Volumetric Shrinkage)**: Modeled around 
$1-$2/mt for standard voyages [13, 14], ensuring VEF (Vessel Experience Factor)
shrinkage across ASTM Table 54 conversions is accounted for in the P&L [15].

If $\Pi_{Arb}(t) > 0$, the algorithmic system should trigger a buy execution on
the physical USGC cargo, lift the prompt MB hedge, and sell the AFEI forward 
swap [16].

### II. Mid-Voyage Arbitrage Collapse & Stochastic Carry-Cost Formula

When the arbitrage window violently collapses mid-voyage—perhaps due to a spike
in US domestic demand or crashing Asian PDH margins sending the crack spread 
negative [17]—the desk must execute a rigorous "Hold vs. Dump" optimization.

If the cargo is dumped, the loss is realized immediately against the depressed 
prompt AFEI spot price. If held, the VLGC is effectively transitioned into a 
Floating Storage Unit (FSU). Floating storage requires maintaining the cargo in
a refrigerated state, which incurs continuous reliquefaction power demands and 
charter hire costs [18]. 

The total floating storage carry cost ($C_{carry}$) from decision time $t$ to 
optimal discharge time $T$ is mathematically defined as:
$$C_{carry}(t, T) = \int_{t}^{T} \left[ TCE_{\tau} + \left(B_{idle} \times 
P_{fuel}(\tau)\right) + (r \times S_{cargo}) \right] d\tau$$

Where:
*   $TCE_{\tau}$: The daily Time Charter Equivalent rate or fixed monthly TC 
cost of the vessel [18].
*   $B_{idle}$: The daily bunker consumption required for idle operations 
(approximately 5 to 10 mt/day) plus the auxiliary engine load required to run 
the reliquefaction plant to keep the cargo cold [11, 18, 19].
*   $P_{fuel}(\tau)$: The prevailing VLSFO or LPG bunker price.
*   $r \times S_{cargo}$: The daily cost of capital tied up in the physical 
cargo.

**The Decision Mechanism (Optimal Stopping Problem):**
To determine whether to hold or dump, treat the floating storage Optionality as
a Bermudan swing or multiple stopping problem [20, 21]. You only transition to 
floating storage if the contango spread (the expected future AFEI price minus 
the current depressed spot price) is strictly greater than the integral of the 
carry cost plus the opportunity cost of missing the next loading window. 

Within the cMotion or gMotion logistics modules of Endur [22], program the 
system to evaluate:
$$E_t[AFEI_{T}] - Spot_{AFEI}(t) \ge C_{carry}(t, T) + \epsilon_{risk}$$
*(Where $\epsilon_{risk}$ is the quantitative risk premium for weather delays 
or boil-off degradation).*

If this inequality holds, instruct Operations to anchor the vessel in 
international waters off Singapore or Chiba [18], roll the short AFEI hedge to 
the deferred month $T$ [23], and log the vessel as "Floating Storage" within 
the CTRM to accurately isolate the Carry component in your daily P&L 
attribution [24, 25].
