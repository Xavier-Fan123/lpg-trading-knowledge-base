### 1. VLGC Market Microstructure
Advanced freight trading requires understanding how ships are actually priced 
and fixed in the physical market. 
*   **The Fixing Process:** Freight rates are negotiated bilaterally through 
international shipbrokers. A vessel is "fixed" when a charterer agrees to terms
with a shipowner, but importantly, a fixture can impact market assessments even
before it is fully concluded (i.e., before "subjects are lifted," meaning final
management or vetting approvals are granted) [Argus Tanker Freight 
methodology]. 
*   **Worldscale vs. Lumpsum:** Freight is often negotiated using the 
"Worldscale" system—a standardized matrix of flat rates—which traders then 
convert to a flat US dollar per tonne ($/t) cost [Argus Tanker Freight 
methodology]. 
*   **Time Charter (TC) vs. Spot:** The market operates on two tiers. Traders 
can secure ships on the spot market for a single voyage, or take them on Time 
Charter (measured in TCE - Time Charter Equivalent earnings, e.g., $80,000/day)
[BW LPG (BWLP) Q4 2025 Earnings Call Transcript]. An independent trading house 
might Time Charter a vessel for 12 months, effectively taking a "long" position
on the freight market, hoping spot rates spike higher than their daily TC cost.
*   **Paper Freight (FFAs):** To manage the extreme volatility of physical 
vessel rates, traders use Forward Freight Agreements (FFAs). These are 
financially settled futures contracts that allow traders to lock in a freight 
rate for a specific route (e.g., Houston-to-Chiba) months in advance [Argus 
Tanker Freight methodology; BW LPG (BWLP) Q4 2025 Earnings Call Transcript].

### 2. Freight Volatility Drivers
Freight spikes are rarely caused by a sudden surge in LPG demand; they are 
almost entirely driven by logistical inefficiencies that artificially shrink 
the supply of ships.
*   **Tonne-Mile Inflation:** The total fleet sits at roughly 421 active VLGCs 
(with 10% being over 25 years old) [BW LPG (BWLP) Q4 2025 Earnings Call 
Transcript]. When vessels are forced to avoid canals and take longer routes, 
the "tonne-mile" demand increases. A ship tied up for an extra 30 days 
round-trip cannot lift a new cargo, instantly tightening the global tonnage 
pool [VLGC market in 2025: A rollercoaster ride with more dips than peaks - 
Drewry].
*   **Canal Chokepoints:** The Panama Canal's Neo-Panamax locks frequently 
operate near maximum capacity. Drought conditions, freshwater surcharges, and 
container ship competition restrict available transit slots for VLGCs [BW LPG 
(BWLP) Q4 2025 Earnings Call Transcript]. 
*   **Geopolitical and War-Risk Premiums:** Tensions in the Middle East 
(specifically the Strait of Hormuz, which handles 34% of global LPG volumes) 
and the Red Sea force massive rerouting. Even if a strait isn't physically 
blocked, the withdrawal of Protection and Indemnity (P&I) war-risk insurance 
effectively closes the route to commercial fleets, forcing vessels to sail via 
the Cape of Good Hope (COGH) [VLGC market in 2025: A rollercoaster ride with 
more dips than peaks - Drewry; US-Iran conflict: Strait of Hormuz crisis 
reshapes global oil markets | Kpler].
*   **Weather Disruptions:** A cold snap or severe fog in the US Gulf Coast can
delay vessel loadings by 5 to 7 days, causing ships to bunch up and temporarily
paralyzing spot chartering activity [VLGC market in 2025: A rollercoaster ride 
with more dips than peaks - Drewry].

### 3. Freight Timing Risk
The mismatch between fixing a vessel and pricing the cargo is where margins are
made or destroyed.
*   **Naked Freight vs. Naked Cargo:** If you buy a physical FOB cargo but wait
to fix a ship, you are "naked" on freight. If the Baltic Houston-Chiba index 
spikes 20% due to a sudden canal delay, your FOB cargo is instantly 
"underwater" (unprofitable) before it even loads [VLGC market in 2025: A 
rollercoaster ride with more dips than peaks - Drewry]. 
*   **Laycan Mismatches and Demurrage:** A VLGC carries roughly 44,000 tonnes 
of refrigerated LPG. Terminals schedule loading windows (laycans) tightly 
because LPG storage utilizes expensive cryogenic steel [Review of the 
appropriateness of the current LPG international benchmark in the setting of 
domestic LPG prices - ACCC]. If your vessel misses its window due to weather, 
you face massive demurrage costs. Conversely, if the ship arrives but the 
terminal's tanks are full ("tank-tops"), upstream refinery production may have 
to be shut in [Review of the appropriateness of the current LPG international 
benchmark in the setting of domestic LPG prices - ACCC].

### 4. Freight Optionality
Advanced traders manage freight risk by building optionality into their 
physical logistics.
*   **Triangular Arbitrage and Cargo Swaps:** Instead of sailing empty (in 
ballast) back to the US, a trader might execute a cargo swap. For instance, 
Japanese traders have historically redirected Canadian volumes to China, 
backfilling their own domestic demand with US cargo. This optimizes the 
vessel's route and maximizes laden (loaded) days versus ballast days [VLGC 
market in 2025: A rollercoaster ride with more dips than peaks - Drewry].
*   **Discharge Optionality:** When fixing a vessel, traders negotiate 
diversion rights. If a vessel loads in the US Gulf targeted for Asia, but the 
Panama Canal backs up and European prices suddenly surge, the trader can 
reroute the VLGC to Rotterdam to capture the better netback [Marine Fuels in 
Transition: Market Dynamics, Future Fuels, and Regulatory Change - OPIS].
*   **The Hengyi Advantage:** For our desk, when Middle East tensions spike and
global vessels are trapped ballasting around the COGH, VLGC effective supply 
crashes [VLGC market in 2025: A rollercoaster ride with more dips than peaks - 
Drewry]. We exercise our structural optionality by utilizing smaller, 
short-haul pressurized or semi-refrigerated ships to target desperate buyers in
South China or Southeast Asia who cannot wait 45 days for a US cargo.

### 5. Freight Sensitivity Model
Senior traders stress-test their arbitrage models daily against specific 
logistical failure points.
*   **The Canal Auction Stress Test:** If the US-to-Asia gross spread is $140/t
*(illustrative)*, the trader must model the base freight cost PLUS the 
probability of a Panama Canal delay. If the waiting queue is 10 days, the 
trader calculates the daily demurrage cost (e.g., $80,000/day * 10 days / 
44,000t = ~$18/t *(illustrative)*) against the cost of paying a massive premium
to win an expedited transit slot auction [Panama Canal Fees for Logistics | 
Cost Guide - FreightAmigo].
*   **The COGH Alternative:** If the canal is blocked, the model must 
dynamically recalculate the netback via the Cape of Good Hope. This adds 10 to 
15 days of voyage time, increasing bunker (fuel) consumption and tying up 
working capital for an extra two weeks, which must be financed at the 
prevailing interest rate [VLGC market in 2025: A rollercoaster ride with more 
dips than peaks - Drewry; BW LPG (BWLP) Q4 2025 Earnings Call Transcript].
