### 1) Core Market Logic
Arbitrage is the engine that drives physical commodity trade. It is the 
practice of capitalizing on geographic or temporal price differences by buying 
a commodity in an oversupplied region and selling it in a high-demand region 
for a profit, after accounting for all logistics costs [1]. 

The global LPG market relies on this mechanism to clear massive surpluses from 
the US Gulf Coast and the Middle East into the primary demand centers of North 
Asia (China, Japan, South Korea) [1, 2]. For traders, the core logic relies on 
the **delivered-cost build-up**. You must perfectly calculate the total cost to
move a molecule from Point A to Point B. If the destination price (e.g., the 
Far East Index / AFEI) is higher than your total delivered cost, the "arb is 
open" [3]. If freight rates spike or canal fees surge, the arb slams shut, and 
trade flows must immediately adapt [1, 4].

### 2) Key Concepts
*   **The Netback Formula:** The calculation a trader uses to determine 
arbitrage viability. $Netback = Price_{Destination} - Freight - Canal Costs - 
Insurance - Terminal Fees$ [3].
*   **Import Parity Price (IPP):** The total landed cost of a commodity at the 
destination. $IPP = Benchmark Price (e.g., Saudi CP) + Freight + Insurance/Loss
+ Port/Terminal Fees + Supplier Margin$ [5].
*   **Freight as the Swing Factor:** VLGC (Very Large Gas Carrier) freight is 
highly volatile and often represents 20-30% of the total delivered cost [6]. It
is the key variable that opens or closes regional arbitrage windows [4, 7].
*   **Canal Optionality & Time Costs:** Routing vessels through the Panama 
Canal incurs tolls, and recent drought restrictions have added massive wait 
times and freshwater surcharges [4]. Rerouting via the Cape of Good Hope adds 
10 to 15 days to the voyage, drastically increasing time costs and tying up 
global shipping capacity [4].
*   **East-West Spread:** The price differential between Asian markets (AFEI) 
and Western markets (Mont Belvieu or CIF ARA). Traders watch this spread 
continuously to determine if US cargoes will flow to Europe or Asia [8, 9].
*   **Tariff & Geopolitical Friction:** Policy changes act as direct arbitrage 
barriers. For example, Chinese tariffs on US LPG created a 10-15% delivered 
economics disadvantage, instantly shifting trade flows toward Middle Eastern 
and Australian suppliers [10, 11]. War risk premiums in the Strait of Hormuz 
can similarly distort Middle Eastern arbitrage [7, 12].

### 3) How Traders Actually Use This
Traders use arbitrage math daily to make 5-minute go/no-go decisions on 
physical cargoes. 

During the "Morning Analysis," a trader calculates the US-to-Asia netback. If 
the spread between AFEI and Mont Belvieu covers freight, canal costs, and fees 
with a healthy margin, the trader immediately calls a broker to secure a US FOB
cargo and a vessel [13, 14]. 

From our Hengyi perspective in Southeast Asia, we constantly run comparative 
netbacks. If the US-to-Asia arb is wide open, we know a flood of US VLGCs is 
heading to China, which will eventually depress the AFEI price. In response, we
might choose to sell our Brunei butane/propane promptly into nearby, 
price-inelastic domestic markets in Southeast Asia rather than competing in the
saturated North Asian spot market [15, 16].

### 4) Step-by-Step Trading Example
Let’s run a comparative arbitrage assessment targeting delivery to South China.

*   **The Destination Price:** CFR South China (AFEI) is pricing at **$650/mt**
*(Illustrative)*.
*   **Option A: US Gulf Coast (USGC) Sourcing:**
    *   Origin Price (Mont Belvieu): $400/mt [3]
    *   Logistics (Freight + Panama Canal Costs + Terminal Fees): $130/mt [3]
    *   *Total Delivered Cost:* $530/mt
    *   *Gross Margin:* **$120/mt** ($650 - $530)
*   **Option B: Middle East Sourcing:**
    *   Origin Price (Saudi CP): $580/mt *(Illustrative)*
    *   Logistics (Freight AG-Japan equivalent + Insurance/Port Fees): $45/mt 
*(Illustrative)*
    *   *Total Delivered Cost:* $625/mt
    *   *Gross Margin:* **$25/mt** ($650 - $625)
*   **The Decision:** The US arb is vastly superior. The trader executes Option
A, locking in the $120/mt spread by buying the physical US cargo, chartering 
the ship, and simultaneously executing paper hedges (Long MB swaps / Short AFEI
swaps) to protect the margin while the vessel is on the water [3, 17].

### 5) Trader Decision Process
**Go:**
*   Calculated netback exceeds internal risk/return hurdles (e.g., > $10/mt 
profit).
*   A vessel is immediately available that perfectly matches the FOB terminal's
laycan (loading window) [18].
*   The East-West paper spread is liquid enough to execute a perfect basis 
hedge before signing the physical contract [6, 17].

**No-Go (Trade Killers):**
*   **Logistics Bottlenecks:** A sudden spike in Panama Canal auction prices or
wait times completely wipes out the US-to-Asia margin [4, 19].
*   **War Risk Surcharges:** Geopolitical escalations in the Middle East (e.g.,
Strait of Hormuz) cause shipowners to refuse voyages or hike insurance 
premiums, killing Middle East export economics [7, 12, 20].
*   **Unhedged Exposure:** You can secure the cargo but cannot secure the 
vessel (or vice versa), leaving you with a naked, unhedged position in a highly
volatile market [6, 21].

### 6) Common Trader Mistakes
*   **Static Cost Assumptions:** Treating port fees, demurrage, and canal 
transit times as fixed numbers. A 5-day delay at a discharge port due to 
terminal congestion can easily eat a $15/mt gross margin in demurrage fees [22,
23].
*   **Ignoring the "Time" Value of Money and Ships:** Failing to account for 
the financial cost of a longer voyage. Rerouting via the Cape of Good Hope adds
10-15 days, which means your capital is tied up longer and you face extended 
market exposure if not perfectly hedged [4, 13].
*   **Trading the Absolute Price Instead of the Spread:** A rookie trader might
buy a US cargo because Mont Belvieu "looks cheap." If AFEI collapses at the 
exact same time, the arb is closed. Professional arbitrageurs trade the 
*relationship* (the basis), not the flat price [1, 17].

### 9) Real-World Signals to Monitor
*   **Panama Canal Wait Times/Auctions:** Directly dictates US-Asia freight 
costs, delivery timelines, and global VLGC supply tightness [4, 24].
*   **VLGC Spot Charter Rates:** Tracked daily; when spot rates spike, 
geographical arbitrage windows narrow rapidly [25, 26].
*   **The East-West Spread:** Monitor the paper differential between AFEI and 
Mont Belvieu (or CIF ARA) to gauge which basin is pulling cargoes [8, 9].
*   **Geopolitical Flashpoints:** News regarding the Strait of Hormuz, which 
immediately impacts war risk insurance premiums and vessel availability [7,
