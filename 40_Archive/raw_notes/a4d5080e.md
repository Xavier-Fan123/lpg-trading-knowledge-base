### 1) Core Market Logic
The global petrochemical industry operates on immense operational leverage [3].
Steam crackers in Northeast Asia are equipped with flexible technology allowing
them to toggle between cracking naphtha and LPG (propane/butane) based on 
prevailing price spreads [1, 3]. 

Because naphtha is derived directly from crude oil, and LPG is heavily supplied
by natural gas processing, their prices often decouple [4]. When LPG becomes 
significantly cheaper than naphtha, flexible crackers aggressively buy LPG 
instead [3]. This sudden surge in petrochemical demand acts as a "floor" for 
LPG prices during oversupplied periods, while the point at which crackers 
switch *back* to naphtha acts as a "price ceiling" that caps LPG's upside [1, 
5]. 

### 2) Key Concepts
*   **The $50 Spread Threshold:** For Asian operators, the critical economic 
threshold for switching from naphtha to LPG is when propane trades at a 
discount of at least $50 per metric ton compared to naphtha [1, 3, 6]. 
*   **The 90% Rule:** Alternatively, traders monitor the ratio: when propane's 
price falls below 90% of the naphtha reference value, it becomes competitively 
attractive for switching [1, 7].
*   **Operational Leverage Multiplier:** Direct feedstock cost savings scale 
non-linearly. Every $10/mt saved in feedstock costs typically translates to 
$15–$20/mt in improved cracker margins (a 1.5x to 2.0x multiplier) [3, 8].
*   **The Pro-Nap Spread (Propane-Naphtha Spread):** The most critical metric 
for cracker economics. If the spread narrows to, say, -$32/mt or -$14/mt, LPG 
is too expensive, and operators will maximize naphtha throughput instead [6, 
9]. 
*   **Switching Friction (24-72 Hours):** Switching isn't instantaneous. It 
requires 24 to 72 hours of operational adjustments, during which plant 
efficiency may temporarily decline [10].
*   **Product Yield Variances:** The choice of feed changes the plant's output.
Naphtha cracking yields more aromatics and gasoline-range hydrocarbons, while 
LPG cracking yields a high ratio of propylene to ethylene [11, 12]. 
*   **Butane vs. Propane Differences:** **Not found in sources** (The sources 
confirm both propane and butane are used in LPG cracking and that widening 
discounts boost both [1, 13], but the *exact economic threshold differences* 
between cracking butane versus propane compared to naphtha are not specified). 
However, traders know butane has a separate, distinct price floor mechanism: 
its high-margin use in winter gasoline blending due to RVP (Reid Vapor 
Pressure) regulations [14].

### 3) How Traders Actually Use This
Traders use the Pro-Nap spread to anticipate massive shifts in physical cargo 
demand before they happen. 

If you see the Pro-Nap spread blow out to a -$80/mt discount, you immediately 
know NE Asian crackers are going to max out their LPG intake [3, 8]. As a 
trader, you get long on the Far East Index (AFEI) paper or secure physical spot
cargoes, knowing this incoming buying wave will drive LPG prices up until the 
spread compresses back toward the $50/mt threshold [1, 6]. 

From an integrated refinery perspective (like our Hengyi operations), if the 
Pro-Nap spread is extremely tight (e.g., -$15/mt), LPG is pricing very strongly
relative to crude/naphtha [15]. We would advise our refinery to feed naphtha 
into the integrated crackers, freeing up our produced LPG to be sold at a 
premium into the spot market [2].

### 4) Step-by-Step Trading Example
Let’s look at a historical arbitrage optimization from November 2025. 
*   **The Setup:** Early in the month, Northwest Europe Naphtha was pricing 
high due to refinery maintenance, while US LPG imports flooded the market [16, 
17]. 
*   **The Spread:** Propane was priced at $450/mt and Naphtha at $570/mt 
(Illustrative prices based on spread data) [18, 19]. The Pro-Nap spread was an 
extreme -$120/mt [18].
*   **The Plant Economics:** A flexible 500,000 ton-per-annum cracker operating
at 75% capacity switches fully to propane. 
*   **The P&L Impact:** The direct feedstock savings are $3.6 million per 
month. Applying the 1.5x–2x operational leverage multiplier, the net monthly 
benefit to the plant's margin is $5.4 to $7.2 million [10, 19].
*   **The Trade:** Knowing this margin will trigger massive LPG buying, a 
trader buys LPG cargoes. By Nov 27, the heavy buying demand (combined with 
returning naphtha supply) collapsed the spread from -$120/mt down to 
-$38.75/mt, netting the trader massive gains on their long LPG position as the 
market rebalanced [18].

### 5) Trader Decision Process
**Go:** 
*   Propane discount to naphtha exceeds $50/mt [1].
*   Price of propane is < 90% of naphtha [1].
*   Downstream margins (like Ethylene and Propylene) are positive, meaning 
crackers actually *want* to run at high utilization rates [20, 21].

**No-Go (Trade Killers):** 
*   **Co-product Collapse:** Even if the Pro-Nap spread looks great, if the 
prices of co-products (butadiene and benzene) have crashed, the *blended* 
cracker margin might still be negative, meaning the plant won't increase run 
rates [22].
*   **Ethane Substitution:** If US ethane is trading at massive discounts, 
crackers that have been retrofitted for ethane will prioritize that over LPG, 
killing your expected LPG demand [23, 24].
*   **Logistics Failures:** If you buy US FOB cargoes to capture this Asian 
demand, but Panama Canal delays spike or VLGC freight rates explode, your 
netback is destroyed before the cargo arrives [25].

### 6) Common Trader Mistakes
*   **Ignoring the Naphtha Crack Spread:** Traders often only look at LPG 
fundamentals. If the naphtha crack spread is weak and naphtha prices collapse, 
it caps any potential upside for LPG, as crackers will instantly switch back to
naphtha [1].
*   **Misjudging Product Yield Economics:** Forgetting that switching to LPG 
drastically drops the yield of aromatics and butadiene [11]. If the market is 
desperately short on butadiene, a cracker might continue running expensive 
naphtha just to harvest the high-value butadiene co-product. 
*   **Underestimating Switching Friction:** Assuming the demand shift is 
instantaneous. It takes 24-72 hours to adjust feed preheating and product 
recovery systems [10], meaning physical spot demand has a slight lag.
