Welcome back to the desk. At this advanced level, we must stop viewing a hedge 
as a perfect shield. In physical trading, executing a paper hedge does not 
eliminate risk—it simply transforms flat-price risk into **basis risk**. 

For our desk at Hengyi, where we manage equity production of butane and propane
out of Brunei, managing basis is how we protect our margin. Our physical 
molecules rarely match the exact parameters of the financial derivatives we 
trade. If you do not actively manage the gaps in location, quality, and timing,
your P&L will bleed out even if you called the macro market direction 
perfectly.

Here is the advanced module on LPG Basis Risk.

***

### 1. Types of Basis Risk
Basis risk is the danger that the price of your physical commodity and the 
price of your financial hedging instrument diverge [1, 2]. In LPG trading, this
manifests in three distinct ways:

*   **Location Basis:** This is the risk that the price relationship between 
the physical market and the trading hub changes [3]. For example, if we sell a 
cargo in South China but hedge it using Mont Belvieu (MB) paper, we are exposed
to the location basis (which includes transatlantic freight and regional 
supply/demand imbalances). If the differential between MB and the Argus Far 
East Index (AFEI) blows out unexpectedly, the hedge will fail to protect the 
physical margin [2].
*   **Product (Quality) Basis:** This occurs when you use a derivative for one 
product to hedge a physically different product [1, 4]. Because butane paper is
notoriously less liquid than propane, traders often cross-hedge physical butane
using propane swaps. However, butane has distinct demand drivers—such as winter
gasoline blending due to RVP (Reid Vapor Pressure) regulations and alkylation 
economics—which can cause butane prices to decouple violently from propane 
[5-9]. 
*   **Timing (Calendar) Basis:** This is the risk that your physical shipment 
schedule or payment terms do not perfectly align with the prompt date of the 
futures or swap contract [2]. If a vessel is delayed, or if your physical 
pricing window is only a few days but your swap settles on a full-month 
average, you have created a severe timing mismatch [2, 10, 11].

### 2. Physical vs Paper Mismatch
Mismatches arise because financial derivatives are standardized, while physical
logistics are inherently messy.
*   **The Monthly Average Trap:** Standard LPG swaps settle against the monthly
average of daily price assessments [10]. However, physical delivery often 
happens in a compressed timeframe. If a trader delivers a physical cargo in the
first week of the month, their physical revenue is locked. If market prices 
collapse in the third week, the monthly average of the financial swap will 
drop, penalizing the trader’s hedge payout. The physical and paper legs are 
fundamentally mismatched [11].
*   **Volume Slippage:** A financial swap is perfectly static (e.g., 
standardized lots). However, physical LPG loaded onto a VLGC expands and 
contracts with temperature, and ships may "short-load" due to tank limits. If 
you hedge exactly 22,000 tonnes, but the ship only safely loads 21,500 tonnes, 
you are left holding 500 tonnes of "naked" (unbacked) paper exposure in a 
volatile market [2].
*   **Posted vs Index Pricing:** If a trader buys physical LPG at a supplier's 
fixed "posted" price but hedges it with an exchange-traded index, they assume 
the risk that the supplier raises their posted price independently of the index
[3, 12].

### 3. Managing Basis Risk
Advanced traders design their deal structures to minimize these residual 
exposures before the cargo even loads:
*   **Index-Linked Contracting:** To eliminate location basis, traders 
negotiate to buy and sell physical LPG at an "index-plus" price rather than a 
fixed posted price. For example, buying physical at the MB daily average plus 
an 8.5-cent differential perfectly locks the physical cost to the same index 
where the financial swap will trade, neutralizing the location basis [12, 13].
*   **Utilizing Financial "Physicals":** To solve the timing mismatch of 
compressed delivery windows, traders use a specific financial tool known as a 
"physical." Unlike a swap that settles on the monthly average, a financial 
physical allows the buyer to choose the exact day of the month to exit the 
position. This allows the trader to match their financial settlement exactly to
the 3-day window when the physical vessel is discharging [11, 14].
*   **Exchange For Physical (EFP):** Traders use EFP procedures to formally 
transition a paper position into a physical delivery agreement with a 
counterparty, effectively netting out the paper risk while securing the 
physical molecule [15, 16].

### 4. Dynamic Hedge Adjustments
A hedge is a living position. As market conditions and physical logistics 
evolve, the hedge must be dynamically adjusted:
*   **Rolling the Hedge:** If our chartered VLGC slips from late January into 
early February due to weather delays, the physical pricing window shifts. The 
trader must immediately execute a "roll"—buying back the January paper and 
selling February paper—paying or receiving the calendar spread to realign the 
timing basis [2, 17].
*   **Delta Hedging & Daily Reconciliation:** Traders run daily reconciliations
comparing their physical inventory against their paper positions [16]. If a 
trader is using options to hedge (e.g., a protective put), they must 
continuously buy or sell underlying futures to remain delta-neutral as the 
option's sensitivity to price changes over time [18].
*   **Flexible Pre-Buys:** To manage extreme price spikes where swaps might not
provide enough coverage, traders combine base swap protection with "non-ratable
pre-buys"—allowing them to pull physical volume at a fixed price on any day 
they choose during a volatile winter season [19, 20].

### 5. Real-World Basis Breakdowns
Basis relationships frequently diverge unexpectedly, forcing traders to absorb 
losses if unmanaged:
*   **The Winter Gasoline Squeeze (Product Basis Blowout):** When the winter 
gasoline blending season begins, refiners maximize butane intake to meet higher
RVP limits [6, 9]. If a trader is holding a physical short position in butane 
(owed to a customer) but hedged it by buying propane paper, a sudden surge in 
gasoline blending demand can cause butane prices to skyrocket while propane 
stays flat. The product basis blows out, and the hedge fails completely [4].
*   **Weather-Driven Location Disconnects:** During the severe Texas freezes 
(e.g., January 2026), local feedgas costs near US Gulf Coast LNG and LPG 
terminals surged to $20-$52/mmBtu as gas was diverted for domestic heating 
[21]. This localized price explosion completely disconnected US Gulf Coast 
physical pricing from the international AFEI and European markets, destroying 
the transatlantic arbitrage basis and inflicting massive losses on traders 
caught holding unhedged location spreads [22-24].
