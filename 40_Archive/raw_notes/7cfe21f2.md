### 1) Core Market Logic
In physical LPG trading, "the price" is just one component of the trade's 
profitability. The Incoterm (FOB, CFR, CIF) dictates who controls the shipping,
who bears the physical risk of the cargo during the voyage, and who captures 
the freight margin [1]. 

Because freight can represent 20-30% of the total delivered cost of an LPG 
cargo [2], controlling the vessel (buying FOB or selling CFR/CIF) allows a 
trader to optimize logistics, play the freight arbitrage, and extract value 
beyond the flat price of the molecule [1, 3]. Conversely, poor structuring 
exposes the desk to massive operational risks—like demurrage delays—that hit 
the P&L directly [4].

### 2) Key Concepts
*   **FOB (Free On Board):** The buyer takes control of the product at the 
loading terminal, brings the vessel, pays for freight, and manages the voyage 
risk [1, 5]. Risk and title transfer from seller to buyer as the goods pass the
vessel's permanent hose connection at the loading port [5, 6].
*   **CFR (Cost and Freight):** The seller provides the vessel, manages 
logistics, and pays freight to the destination port [1, 7, 8]. However, **risk 
transfers to the buyer** the moment the goods cross the ship's rail at the 
loading port [9, 10]. 
*   **CIF (Cost, Insurance, and Freight):** Identical to CFR, but the seller is
also obligated to procure marine insurance (typically for 110% of the cargo 
value) against voyage risks [1, 8, 11]. 
*   **Nomination & Vetting:** The buyer (in FOB) or seller (in CFR/CIF) 
"nominates" a specific VLGC and a loading window. The terminal operator must 
accept the vessel after "vetting" it for safety and technical standards [12].
*   **Laycan (Laydays & Canceling Date):** The specific window of days during 
which the vessel must arrive and tender its Notice of Readiness (NOR) at the 
terminal [13, 14]. 
*   **Demurrage & Deadfreight:** If a vessel sits at anchor waiting for a berth
or cargo, demurrage is charged (often $50,000+/day, hitting P&L directly) [4, 
15]. Deadfreight is the penalty paid if the vessel sails partially empty 
because the agreed cargo volume wasn't available [16].
*   **Documentary Settlement (LCs & LOIs):** Payment is typically settled via a
Letter of Credit (LC) requiring a complete set of clean Bills of Lading (BL), 
Certificates of Origin, and independent inspection reports (e.g., SGS) [12, 
17]. If the physical BL is delayed, a Letter of Indemnity (LOI) is used to 
discharge the cargo [18, 19].

### 3) How Traders Actually Use This
Traders use Incoterms to actively manage their P&L attribution, unbundling a 
trade into flat price, basis, and freight [20, 21]. 

As a refinery-linked trading desk (Hengyi), we generate natural long positions 
of LPG. If we sell **FOB Brunei**, we transfer the freight risk and logistics 
headaches to the buyer. This is clean and low-risk, but it means we give up the
freight arbitrage. Large independent trading houses (like Vitol or Gunvor) love
buying FOB because they manage large VLGC fleets and can capture geographical 
arbitrage by optimizing the vessel's route [1, 3].

If we want to capture that value ourselves, we sell **CFR or CIF to North 
Asia**. We time-charter our own vessels, absorb the logistics management, and 
capture the spread between the physical freight cost and the freight premium 
embedded in the CFR sale price. If we are selling to smaller, price-sensitive 
buyers or "teapot" petrochemical plants in China or Southeast Asia, we often 
use **CIF** so that we retain control over the marine insurance, mitigating 
counterparty default risk if the ship is damaged [1, 22].

### 4) Step-by-Step Trading Example
Let’s look at a "Back-to-Back Physical" trade that strips out flat price risk 
to purely capture basis and freight margin [23].

*   **The Setup:** You spot a supply glut at an independent terminal in 
Southeast Asia and strong demand in South China.
*   **The Buy Leg (FOB):** You negotiate to buy 22,000 mt of LPG FOB at the 
terminal. Price: AFEI - $20/mt *(Illustrative)*.
*   **The Sell Leg (CFR):** You simultaneously sell 22,000 mt CFR South China. 
Price: AFEI + $5/mt *(Illustrative)*. 
*   **The Freight:** You use a time-chartered vessel from our fleet that costs 
$15/mt for this route *(Illustrative)*.
*   **P&L Attribution:** 
    *   Gross physical spread = $25/mt.
    *   Minus $15/mt freight = **$10/mt net margin** *(Illustrative)*. 
    *   Because both sides are priced against the AFEI floating index, the flat
price is perfectly neutralized [23]. Your $10/mt profit comes purely from your 
execution of logistics and geographic basis.

### 5) Trader Decision Process
**Go:**
*   Vessel vetting is fully approved by the loading and discharge terminals 
[12].
*   Buyer provides an operative Letter of Credit (LC) or Standby LC (SBLC) from
a prime bank before loading commences [12, 24].
*   Independent inspector (e.g., SGS) confirms the cargo is perfectly "on-spec"
(correct propane/butane ratio, sulfur limits) [12, 17].

**No-Go (Trade Killers):**
*   **Credit Line Exhaustion:** The buyer cannot post the LC, leaving you 
exposed to total default [12, 24].
*   **Off-Spec Product:** If the SGS report shows high moisture or sulfur, 
high-precision PDH plants will reject it, destroying the cargo's value [12].
*   **Laycan Mismatch:** If your FOB purchase laycan doesn't perfectly align 
with your vessel's arrival schedule, you face massive demurrage risks or 
cancellation [4].

### 6) Common Trader Mistakes
*   **Misunderstanding Risk Transfer in CFR:** Assuming the seller holds the 
risk until the ship arrives. In CFR, risk transfers to the buyer at the loading
port rail [9, 10]. If the ship's reliquefaction plant fails mid-voyage and the 
cargo warms/vents [4], the *buyer* takes the loss and must claim their own 
insurance. 
*   **Volume Slippage:** Hedging exactly 22,000 mt on paper, but the physical 
vessel only loads 21,500 mt due to temperature expansion or tank limits. The 
remaining 500 mt leaves the trader over-hedged (naked short) [20].
*   **Sloppy Documentation:** Failing to secure a clean Bill of Lading or a 
perfectly matched SGS Certificate of Quality/Quantity. Any discrepancy stops 
the bank from releasing funds under the LC, freezing millions in working 
capital [12, 17].

### 8) Answer Framework
1.  **Analyze the Risk Gap:** Identify the specific flaw in the buyer's 
requested CFR term regarding voyage risk and insurance capability.
2.  **Select the Incoterm:** Choose the term that allows us to control the 
insurance policy, ensuring that if the cargo is lost, a reliable underwriter 
pays us (since the buyer's credit is weak).
3.  **Specify Financial Security:** Name the exact bank instrument required to 
eliminate counterparty payment default before the ship loads.
4.  **Calculate P&L:** Determine the gross spread (Premium minus Freight and 
Insurance costs) to confirm the trade remains profitable. 

### 9) Real-World Signals to Monitor
*   **Daily Reconciliation:** Comparing physical positions against paper hedges
by product, location, and month to catch volume slippage [25].
*   **VLGC Spot Freight Rates:** If spot freight spikes, CFR/CIF netbacks 
shrink, making FOB sales relatively more attractive [2, 26].
*   **Panama/Suez Canal Congestion:** Wait times tighten vessel supply, drive 
up freight costs, and threaten laycan windows [26].
