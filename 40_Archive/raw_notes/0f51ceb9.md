### 1) Core Market Logic
In LPG trading, P&L is generated by moving molecules from regions of structural
oversupply to regions of structural deficit. Because the majority of global LPG
is produced as a byproduct of natural gas processing rather than targeted 
extraction, supply is highly inelastic to LPG prices ("The Professional LPG 
Trader’s Knowledge Handbook", Sec: Primary Supply Regions and Production 
Drivers [1]). When a commodity *must* be produced regardless of its price, any 
sudden shift in demand (like a cold snap in Japan or a PDH plant outage in 
China) creates violent price disconnects between regions. Your job as a trader 
is to identify these disconnects and execute geographical arbitrages (moving 
it) or feedstock arbitrages (cracking it) before the market corrects.

### 2) Key Concepts
*   **Must-Take Production:** 55.1% of global LPG comes from non-associated 
natural gas processing ("Liquefied Petroleum Gas Market Size | Industry Report,
2033", Sec: Source Insights [2]). If a US shale producer is pumping natural gas
to meet power demand, the NGLs (LPG) *must* be stripped out and sold, even if 
LPG prices are crashing, creating periods of massive oversupply ("The 
Professional LPG Trader’s Knowledge Handbook", Sec: Primary Supply Regions and 
Production Drivers [1]).
*   **Propane vs. Butane Demand Decoupling:** Never trade them as identical 
twins. Propane demand is heavily driven by winter heating and dedicated Propane
Dehydrogenation (PDH) plants in China ("The Professional LPG Trader’s Knowledge
Handbook", Sec: Demand Centers and the Petrochemical Pivot [3]). Butane demand 
is heavily driven by seasonal gasoline blending due to Reid Vapor Pressure 
(RVP) rules, peaking in winter and dropping off sharply in summer when ozone 
regulations change ("The Professional LPG Trader’s Knowledge Handbook", Sec: 
Butane Blending Strategies [4]).
*   **The Chinese PDH Baseline:** China is the market's center of gravity, 
consuming over 96 million tonnes annually, largely driven by PDH plants 
("Global LPG Statistics (2025 Data, Growth & Forecast)", Sec: China [5, 6]). 
These plants provide the "swing demand" that sets the floor and ceiling for the
Argus Far East Index (AFEI) ("The Professional LPG Trader’s Knowledge 
Handbook", Sec: Demand Centers and the Petrochemical Pivot [3]).
*   **Southeast Asia's Structural Deficit:** A structural deficit occurs when 
domestic production permanently falls below baseline consumption. While 
historically an exporter, SEA domestic production from mature fields (Malaysia,
Indonesia) is entering natural decline, projecting the region to become a net 
importer by 2027 ("Asia-Pacific Natural Gas Liquids and Refining Outlook 
2026-2027", Sec: Southeast Asia: Origin Constraints [7]). 
*   **The Netback Equation:** The formula used to determine if a trade makes 
money. Netback = Destination Price - Freight - Canal/Transit Costs - Insurance 
- Terminal Fees ("The Professional LPG Trader’s Knowledge Handbook", Sec: Mont 
Belvieu (MB) and the US Netback [8]). 

### 3) How Traders Actually Use This
On the desk, you don't just read supply/demand reports; you translate them into
trade hypotheses. For example, if you see US natural gas production hitting 
record highs, you know a wave of "must-take" LPG is coming to the Gulf Coast. 
If you simultaneously see Chinese PDH margins turning positive, you form an 
"Arb Hypothesis": The US will be forced to export, and China will aggressively 
buy. You check the VLGC freight rates, and if the logistics cost is lower than 
the price spread between Mont Belvieu and South China, you buy the physical 
cargo FOB Houston and hedge it by selling AFEI paper. 

For your desk's specific focus (SEA → China Butane), you are looking at 
intra-regional flows. Because SEA is transitioning into a net importer, legacy 
export facilities like Bintulu are becoming less reliable as "baseload" 
suppliers ("Asia-Pacific Natural Gas Liquids and Refining Outlook 2026-2027", 
Sec: Conclusions on SEA Origin Constraints and Security [9]). You will use 
Singapore's Jurong hub for storage and blending to capture localized price 
spikes when Chinese buyers get caught short on butane ("Southeast Asia Oil and 
Gas Market Analysis", Sec: Market Trends [10, 11]).

### 4) Step-by-Step Trading Example
Let's build a basic geographical arbitrage model for moving a cargo from the US
Gulf Coast to Asia. 
*Note: Numbers are illustrative based on textbook formulas in the sources.*

1.  **Identify Destination Price:** The Argus Far East Index (AFEI) is trading 
at $550/tonne (illustrative).
2.  **Identify Source Price:** Mont Belvieu (MB) is trading at $400/tonne 
(illustrative).
3.  **Calculate the Spread:** AFEI minus MB = $150/tonne spread.
4.  **Calculate Logistics:** Total freight + terminal fees + insurance = 
$130/tonne (illustrative).
5.  **Calculate Netback / Arb Margin:** $150 Spread - $130 Logistics = 
**+$20/tonne Arb Profit** ("The Professional LPG Trader’s Knowledge Handbook", 
Sec: Mont Belvieu (MB) and the US Netback [8]).

Since the margin is positive ($+20/mt), the "Arb is open." You buy the FOB 
cargo, charter the vessel, and lock in the paper spread. 

### 5) Trader Decision Process
*   **Go:** The Netback calculation is positive, PDH run rates in China are 
>75% (indicating strong physical demand), and freight rates are stable ("The 
Professional LPG Trader’s Knowledge Handbook", Sec: Key Indicators and Daily 
Reports [12]).
*   **No-Go / Kill the Trade:** A sudden spike in VLGC freight rates wipes out 
the margin. Or, Panama Canal congestion forces your vessel to reroute via the 
Cape of Good Hope, adding 10-15 days of voyage time and destroying your 
delivery window economics ("The Professional LPG Trader’s Knowledge Handbook", 
Sec: The Role of VLGC Shipping in Global Arbitrage [13]).
*   **Risk Management Check:** Are we hedged on the exact loading month vs. 
delivery month to avoid time-structure (contango/backwardation) basis risk? 

### 6) Common Trader Mistakes
*   **Treating LPG purely as an oil derivative:** While it has a 60-70% 
correlation to Brent Crude, LPG is heavily driven by natural gas extraction 
("The Professional LPG Trader’s Knowledge Handbook", Sec: LPG vs. LNG and Crude
[14]). If oil rallies but US natural gas is oversupplied, your LPG long 
position might bleed out.
*   **Ignoring Butane RVP blending dates:** A common mistake for new traders is
holding long physical butane positions into late April. On April 28, the US 
summer ozone season begins, refiners strip butane out of the gasoline pool, and
the sudden supply glut crashes the price ("The Professional LPG Trader’s 
Knowledge Handbook", Sec: Butane Blending Strategies [4]).

### 8) Answer Framework
*   **Step 1: Evaluate Feedstock Substitution:** Compare the skyrocketing 
naphtha price to LPG. If propane/butane is >$50/mt cheaper, Chinese crackers 
will aggressively bid for your butane ("The Professional LPG Trader’s Knowledge
Handbook", Sec: Feedstock Substitution: The LPG vs. Naphtha Spread [15]).
*   **Step 2: Evaluate Seasonal Supply:** Recognize that mid-April means US 
refineries are purging butane for summer RVP compliance ("The Professional LPG 
Trader’s Knowledge Handbook", Sec: Butane Blending Strategies [4]). US exports 
will surge, but they are weeks away from China.
*   **Step 3: Evaluate Regional Premium:** The Bintulu outage creates an 
immediate localized shortage in SEA ("Asia-Pacific Natural Gas Liquids and 
Refining Outlook 2026-2027", Sec: Conclusions on SEA Origin Constraints and 
Security [9]).
*   **Action:** Sell your prompt SEA physical butane at a massive premium to 
desperate Chinese crackers, and buy deferred-month AFEI paper to catch the 
incoming wave of cheap US butane in May/June.
