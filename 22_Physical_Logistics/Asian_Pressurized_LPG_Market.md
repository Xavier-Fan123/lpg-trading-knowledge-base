# Asian Pressurized LPG Market

The Asian pressurized LPG market encompasses the trade of liquefied petroleum gas via fully pressurized and semi-pressurized gas carriers (PGCs), typically in the **500–6,000 m3** vessel segment (industry classification: **coasters**, i.e. <15,000 m3). This market serves as the "last mile" distribution layer beneath the VLGC bulk trade, delivering small parcels (2,000–4,000 MT) to terminals that lack refrigerated receiving infrastructure.

## Fleet Landscape

### Tier 1: Global Leaders with Asian Presence

| Owner | Fleet Size | Segment | Asian Presence |
|-------|-----------|---------|----------------|
| **BW Epic Kosan** (Singapore) | 48-49 vessels, 3,000-11,000 cbm | Pressurized + semi-ref + ethylene | **Dominant** — HQ in Singapore, offices Manila/Tokyo |
| **StealthGas** (Athens) | 29 vessels, 3,000-8,000 cbm | Fully pressurized | **Minimal** — only 3 vessels East of Suez; Asia rates too low |
| **Exmar** (Antwerp) | 10 pressurized + 1 semi-pressurized | Pressurized | **Moderate** — 6 vessels in Far East via Wah Kwong JV |

### Tier 2: Handysize / Semi-Ref (Adjacent Segment)

|-------|-----------|---------|------|

| **Solvang ASA** (Norway) | ~20 vessels | Semi-ref / ethylene | Competitor in mid-size segment |

### Tier 3: Regional/National Operators

| Owner | Region | Note |
|-------|--------|------|
| **Pertamina International Shipping** | Indonesia | 102 tankers, 7 VLGCs — domestic VLGC distribution |
| **Petredec** (Singapore) | SE Asia → shifted to VLGC | Exited small pressurized; now VLGC-focused |
| **Regional private owners** | China, India, Indonesia | Numerous 1-5 vessel operators on domestic coastal routes |

### Key Insight: Western vs Eastern Rate Arbitrage

StealthGas — the world's largest pressurized fleet owner — deliberately limits Asian deployment to just 3 vessels because **East of Suez pressurized rates are substantially below Western (Mediterranean/North Sea) rates**. This creates a structural supply gap in Asia, where BW Epic Kosan dominates by default with its Singapore-headquartered operation.

StealthGas financial profile confirms sector economics: FY2024 revenue $167.3M / net income $69.9M (record); FY2025 revenue $173.2M / net income $60.6M. Company repaid all $350M debt since Dec 2022, all vessels unencumbered — demonstrating that pressurized LPG shipping is highly profitable in the right (Western) markets, but operators actively avoid Asia due to rate differential.

## Vessel Economics

### Pressurized vs Refrigerated Cost Comparison

| Factor | Pressurized Coaster (3,000-5,000 cbm) | VLGC (84,000+ cbm) |
|--------|---------------------------------------|---------------------|
| Cargo per voyage | 2,000-4,000 MT | 44,000 MT |
| Per-tonne freight | $40-80/mt (short-haul) | $15-25/mt (long-haul) |
| Terminal investment | $5-20M | $50-200M+ |
| Refrigeration required | No | Yes |
| Boil-off loss | None | Yes |
| Berth requirements | Simple jetty | Deep-water, vapor recovery |
| Build cost | Lower | $90-100M+ per newbuild |

### When Pressurized Wins

Pressurized delivery is competitive when:
1. **Short distance** (<1,000 nm) — freight disadvantage per tonne is minimized
2. **Small demand center** (<5,000 MT/month) — VLGC parcel too large
3. **No cold storage** at receiving terminal — only option
4. **Byproduct economics** — seller (e.g. Hengyi PMB) has tank-top pressure to move volumes quickly at aggressive pricing

### Pressurized-Dependent Markets (No Refrigerated Terminal)

- **Bangladesh** — Mongla and Chattogram ports; entirely dependent on pressurized cargoes; **1.45 million tonnes** imported in 2025 (up from 1.24M in 2023)
- **Myanmar** — emerging market, limited infrastructure; main suppliers Indonesia, Thailand, Singapore; average import price $873/mt (2024)
- **Philippine islands** — geography creates natural small-parcel demand; Petron holds 25.1% LPG market share (2025)
- **Sri Lanka** — LAUGFS operates South Asia's largest LPG transshipment terminal (30,000 MT storage, Hambantota); H1 2024 throughput 149,980 mt (+91% YoY); **60% is re-exported** to regional markets

## Terminal Infrastructure

### India: MGC-to-VLGC Transition

India historically relied on MGC-sized terminals. Key developments:
- **Pipavav** (Gujarat): Commenced VLGC operations; Aegis Vopak expansion
- **JNPT**: BW LPG planning 120,000 cbm storage terminal for 93,000 cbm VLGCs
- **Kandla, Dahej**: Major western coast import terminals
- Pipeline network linking western terminals to 22 LPG bottling plants across Gujarat, Madhya Pradesh, Uttar Pradesh
- India's total LPG storage capacity: ~1.9 million tonnes (only 22 days of supply)

### South China

Multiple small pressurized terminals along Guangdong, Guangxi, Hainan coastline — natural market for Hengyi PMB pressurized output. Also served by large VLGC terminals (Dongguan, Zhanjiang) for breakbulk redistribution.

### Bangladesh

LPG imports handled entirely by private companies via Chattogram and Mongla ports:
- Import volume: **1.24M mt** (2023) → **1.44M mt** (2024) → **1.45M mt** (2025)
- Beximco LPG operates 3,000 MT storage at Mongla; recorded no direct imports since 2025
- Bashundhara LP Gas share collapsed from 15.5% (FY2023) to 3.68% (FY2025) after 2024 political changeover
- Emerging players gaining ground as major importers sidelined
- No refrigerated terminal — entirely pressurized-dependent market

### Sri Lanka (LAUGFS Hambantota Hub)

- 30,000 MT storage capacity at Hambantota International Port — largest LPG transshipment terminal in South Asia
- Can receive both VLGCs and pressurized vessels; stores propane/butane separately or mixed
- H1 2024 throughput: 149,980 mt (91% increase YoY)
- Capacity turns over 3x per month; **60% of volume is re-exported** — functions as a regional redistribution hub
- Re-export model: import via VLGC, break into pressurized parcels, redistribute to South Asian/Indian Ocean markets

## Competitive Dynamics: Three Distribution Models

### 1. VLGC Hub-and-Spoke (Dominant for Major Markets)
VLGC delivers 44,000 MT to hub terminal → breakbulk into trucks/pipeline/coasters
- **Where**: China (Dongguan, Ningbo, Zhanjiang), India (Pipavav, Kandla), Japan, Korea
- **Economics**: Lowest per-tonne freight ($15-25/mt), highest terminal investment

### 2. Semi-Refrigerated Regional (Growing Fastest)
15,000-50,000 cbm vessels on intra-regional routes
- **Where**: Intra-Asia medium-haul, Europe-Med
- **Economics**: Balance of scale and flexibility; CAGR 15.1%

### 3. Direct Pressurized Delivery (Niche but Essential)
500-6,000 cbm vessels deliver directly to end-market
- **Where**: Bangladesh, Myanmar, Philippine islands, South China small ports, Sri Lanka
- **Economics**: Highest per-tonne freight ($40-80/mt), lowest terminal investment ($5-20M)
- **Structural advantage**: Only option for markets lacking refrigerated infrastructure

### Market Evolution Pattern

Petredec's trajectory illustrates the lifecycle: initially dependent on small pressurized cargoes in SE Asia → as markets matured and terminals upgraded → shifted to larger refrigerated/VLGC fleet. This pattern repeats as developing markets build refrigerated infrastructure, but **new frontier markets continuously emerge** (Myanmar, Bangladesh interior, Philippine secondary ports).

## Associated Risks

- **Rate compression**: Asian pressurized rates significantly below Western markets → limited fleet supply from global owners like StealthGas
- **Infrastructure obsolescence**: Terminal upgrades enabling larger vessels erode pressurized trade volumes in maturing markets
- **Hormuz chokepoint**: 90% of India's LPG imports transit Strait of Hormuz; disruption ripples into pressurized redistribution
- **Byproduct supply variability**: Hengyi PMB and other refinery-sourced pressurized supply fluctuates with crude throughput, not LPG market conditions — see [[Hengyi Industries]]
- **Regulatory pressure**: IMO 2028 carbon framework raises operating costs for older pressurized tonnage
- **Tariff disruption**: US-China tariffs redirecting VLGC trade flows alter hub-and-spoke patterns, affecting downstream pressurized redistribution
