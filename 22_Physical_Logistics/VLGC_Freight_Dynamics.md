## Vessel Specifications

The **Very Large Gas Carrier (VLGC)** is the workhorse of the seaborne LPG trade. Key specifications:

- **Cargo capacity**: 44,000-47,000 metric tonnes (MT) of LPG
- Fully refrigerated storage at approximately **-42 degrees C** (propane) and **-1 degree C** (butane) in cryogenic/semi-refrigerated tanks
- Typical vessel dimensions: ~225m LOA, 36m beam
- Draft considerations critical for [[Panama Canal]] transit (tropical fresh water draft limits)
- Modern vessels are dual-fuel (LPG as fuel) reducing bunker costs by 15-20%

## Global Fleet

The global VLGC fleet stands at approximately **421 vessels** (including newbuilds on order), making it a relatively concentrated market:

- Fleet growth has been driven by US LPG export expansion since 2015
- Average fleet age: ~8-10 years
- Newbuild delivery cycle: 2-3 years from order, creating supply response lags
- Scrapping threshold: typically 25+ years, accelerated by environmental regulations (CII, EEXI)

## Freight as a Cost Component

VLGC freight represents **20-30% of the delivered cost** of LPG from the US Gulf Coast to Asia, making it the single largest variable cost in [[Geographical Arbitrage]]:

| Route | Distance (nm) | Typical Duration | Freight Share of Delivered Cost |
|-------|---------------|-----------------|-------------------------------|
| USGC - Japan (via Panama) | ~9,500 | 25-30 days | 20-25% |
| USGC - Japan (via Cape) | ~15,500 | 40-50 days | 25-30% |
| AG - Japan | ~6,500 | 18-22 days | 15-20% |

## Tonne-Mile Demand Inflation

A critical concept: **tonne-mile demand** can increase even if volumetric LPG trade is flat. When [[Panama Canal]] congestion or drought forces cargoes via the [[Cape of Good Hope Route]], the same cargo consumes ~60% more vessel-days, tightening the fleet and inflating rates. This is the primary mechanism through which canal disruptions transmit into freight spikes.

## Spot Charter Rate Mechanics

VLGC spot rates are quoted in **USD per metric tonne** on benchmark routes (e.g., AG-Japan, USGC-Japan):

- **Spot rate volatility**: Rates can swing from $30/mt to $120+/mt within a single quarter
- **Time charter equivalent (TCE)**: Converts voyage economics to a daily rate for comparison (~$15,000-80,000/day range)
- Rate drivers: fleet utilization, bunker costs, canal availability, seasonal demand, ton-mile effects
- **Baltic LPG Index**: Published benchmark for VLGC freight rates

## Cryogenic Infrastructure Requirements

Loading and discharging VLGCs requires specialized infrastructure:

- Refrigerated terminal storage tanks with boil-off gas (BOG) management
- High-capacity loading arms rated for cryogenic service
- Vapor return lines to manage tank pressure during loading
- Cool-down procedures for tanks transitioning between grades

Freight dynamics are integral to [[Geographical Arbitrage]] economics, [[Demurrage and Laytime]] calculations, and [[Physical Hedging Architecture]] as freight exposure must be managed alongside flat price risk.

## Empirical Facts vs Analytical Assumptions

### Empirical Facts
- Global VLGC fleet stands at approximately 421 vessels including newbuilds on order
- VLGC cargo capacity ranges from 44,000-47,000 metric tonnes
- VLGC spot rates have historically swung from $30/mt to $120+/mt within a single quarter
- USGC-Japan via Panama is approximately 9,500 nautical miles; via Cape approximately 15,500 nautical miles
- Panama Canal rerouting via Cape of Good Hope adds approximately 60% more vessel-days to the voyage
- Modern dual-fuel VLGCs reduce bunker costs by 15-20% compared to conventional vessels
- Freight represents 20-30% of the delivered cost of LPG from USGC to Asia
- Baltic LPG Index is a published benchmark for VLGC freight rates
- CII and EEXI environmental regulations are accelerating scrapping of older vessels
- Newbuild delivery cycle is 2-3 years from order

### Analytical Assumptions
- Fleet growth will continue to be driven primarily by US LPG export expansion
- Dual-fuel adoption will become the standard for newbuilds, structurally lowering bunker cost exposure
- Panama Canal congestion events will recur with increasing frequency, making tonne-mile demand inflation a persistent structural feature
- Scrapping threshold of 25+ years may compress further under tightening environmental regulation
- TCE range of $15,000-80,000/day captures the full cycle; sustained rates above $60,000/day incentivize newbuild orders
- Freight volatility will remain elevated relative to flat price volatility due to the concentrated fleet structure

## Scenario Analysis

### Base Case
- Fleet growth of 3-5% annually broadly matches demand growth from US export expansion and Asian import pull
- Freight rates oscillate within $40-80/mt on the USGC-Japan route, with seasonal peaks in Q4 (winter demand) and Q1
- Panama Canal operates with periodic draft restrictions but no prolonged closures
- Tonne-mile demand grows moderately as Atlantic-to-Pacific flows remain dominant

### Bull Case
- Prolonged Panama Canal disruption (drought, infrastructure failure) forces sustained Cape rerouting, absorbing 15-20% of effective fleet capacity
- Simultaneous demand surge from Asian PDH capacity additions and cold Northern Hemisphere winter
- Newbuild deliveries delayed by shipyard bottlenecks, constraining fleet supply response
- Rates spike above $100/mt on benchmark routes, with TCE exceeding $70,000/day

### Bear Case
- Global LPG demand contraction from economic recession or accelerated energy transition reduces seaborne volumes
- Massive newbuild wave delivers simultaneously, causing fleet oversupply
- Panama Canal operates at full capacity with no congestion, reducing tonne-mile demand
- Rates collapse below $30/mt, pushing older vessels to scrapping and new orders to zero

### Invalidation Triggers
- Structural shift in LPG sourcing (e.g., Middle East displaces US as marginal supplier to Asia) would collapse the long-haul tonne-mile thesis
- Development of alternative transport infrastructure (pipeline or land-bridge) bypassing seaborne routes
- Permanent Panama Canal expansion eliminating transit bottlenecks
- Regulatory change banning LPG as marine fuel, removing dual-fuel cost advantage

## Downside Risks

- **Freight spike exposure**: Unhedged freight positions during canal disruptions can erode or eliminate cargo margins entirely; see [[VLGC Freight Dynamics]] rate volatility history
- **Tonne-mile demand miscalculation**: Overestimating rerouting probability leads to incorrect fleet utilization assumptions; linked to [[Geographical Arbitrage]] netback errors
- **Demurrage compounding**: Congestion at load/discharge ports during high-freight periods creates double cost exposure through both elevated rates and [[Demurrage and Laytime]] charges
- **Basis risk on freight hedging**: Paper freight instruments (FFAs) may not perfectly track physical voyage economics; see [[Basis Risk Management]]
- **Newbuild cycle timing**: Ordering vessels at cycle peaks locks in high capital costs before potential rate collapses
- **Environmental regulation risk**: Accelerated CII/EEXI enforcement could strand non-compliant vessels, creating sudden fleet composition shifts
