## Delivery Terms: FOB / CIF / CFR / DES

Physical LPG contracts are structured around [[Incoterms in LPG Trading|Incoterms]] that define risk transfer, cost allocation, and operational responsibilities:

- **FOB (Free on Board)**: Seller delivers LPG to the vessel at the loading port. Risk and title transfer at the **hose connection** (or flange connection) at the load port. Buyer arranges and pays for the vessel.
- **CFR (Cost and Freight)**: Seller arranges and pays for freight to the discharge port. However, risk still transfers at the **load port hose connection**. Buyer bears risk during transit despite not controlling the vessel.
- **CIF (Cost, Insurance, and Freight)**: Same as CFR but the seller also procures marine cargo insurance for the buyer's account.
- **DES (Delivered Ex-Ship)**: Seller bears all costs and risks until the cargo is delivered alongside the vessel at the discharge port. Maximum seller exposure.

## Risk vs Title Transfer

A critical legal nuance: in LPG trading, **risk transfer occurs at the hose connection** at the load port for FOB, CFR, and CIF terms. This is the moment when:

- The seller's liability for cargo loss/damage ends
- The buyer's insurance coverage must commence
- Quality and quantity are typically measured at this point via shore tank gauging or flow meters

## Independent Inspection

Third-party inspection (typically by **SGS**, Intertek, or Saybolt) is standard practice:

- **Quantity determination**: Shore tank gauging (before/after), flow meter readings, vessel tank surveys (Cargo Measurement Tables)
- **Quality testing**: Specific gravity, vapor pressure, composition analysis (C3/C4 split), sulfur content, moisture
- **Manifest error exception**: A recognized contractual provision allowing for minor discrepancies (typically 0.5%) between loaded and discharged quantities due to measurement uncertainty, temperature effects, and boil-off during transit

## Off-Spec and Contamination Risk

LPG quality risks include:

- **Cross-contamination**: Residual cargo from previous voyage if tanks not properly cleaned/purged
- **Moisture content**: Water in propane can cause hydrate formation and freeze valves
- **H2S contamination**: Particularly from Middle East origin cargoes; spec is typically <3 ppm
- **Off-spec consequences**: Buyer may reject cargo, claim price discount, or require blending. Seller liable for re-nomination or alternative supply

## LPG Product Quality Specifications

Standard commercial propane specifications (reference: California Code Regs. Tit. 13, § 2292.6; ASTM test methods):

| Parameter | Spec | Test Method |
|-----------|------|-------------|
| Propane content | ≥85.0 vol% | ASTM D 2163 |
| Vapor pressure @100°F | ≤208 psig | ASTM D 1267 / D 2598 |
| Volatility (95% evap. temp) | ≤-37°F | ASTM D 1837 |
| Butanes | ≤5.0 vol% | ASTM D 2163 |
| Butenes | ≤2.0 vol% | ASTM D 2163 |
| Pentenes and heavier | ≤0.5 vol% | ASTM D 2163 |
| Propene | ≤10.0 vol% | ASTM D 2163 |
| Residue on evap. (100 ml) | ≤0.05 ml | ASTM D 2158 |
| Copper strip corrosion | No. 1 max | ASTM D 1838 |
| Total sulfur | ≤80 ppmw | ASTM D 2784 |
| Moisture content | pass | ASTM D 2713 |

**Trading relevance**: Off-spec cargoes (e.g., high sulfur, excess olefins) face price discounts or rejection. PDH feedstock requires tighter specs on C4+ content. Middle East cargoes may carry elevated H2S. Buyers typically specify quality in the Sales & Purchase Agreement referencing GPA/ASTM standards.

## Reliquefaction Failure

During transit, LPG cargo generates **boil-off gas (BOG)** as heat ingress causes evaporation:

- VLGC reliquefaction plants must maintain cargo temperature and pressure
- **Reliquefaction failure** leads to cargo losses (vented or used as fuel) and potential pressure buildup
- Contractual responsibility depends on Incoterm: under CFR/CIF, the seller (as charterer) typically bears this risk indirectly through the charter party

## Back-to-Back Structures

The dominant risk management structure in physical LPG trading:

- **Buy FOB** at origin on one contract, **sell CFR or CIF** at destination on another
- Ideally with matching pricing formulas (both referencing [[AFEI Benchmark]])
- The trader's margin is the **spread** between the two contracts minus freight and operational costs
- Back-to-back structures minimize flat price risk but retain basis, timing, and operational risks

See [[Demurrage and Laytime]] for time-related contract mechanics and [[Incoterms in LPG Trading]] for detailed Incoterm analysis.

## Empirical Facts vs Analytical Assumptions

### Empirical Facts
- Risk transfer under FOB, CFR, and CIF occurs at the load port hose connection point
- DES transfers risk at the discharge port
- Independent inspection by SGS, Intertek, or Saybolt is standard practice in physical LPG trade
- Manifest error exception typically allows 0.5% discrepancy between loaded and discharged quantities
- H2S contamination specification is typically <3 ppm, particularly for Middle East origin cargoes
- VLGC reliquefaction plants must manage boil-off gas (BOG) to maintain cargo temperature and pressure during transit
- Back-to-back structures (buy FOB at origin, sell CFR/CIF at destination) are the dominant risk management structure
- Quality testing includes specific gravity, vapor pressure, C3/C4 composition analysis, sulfur content, and moisture

### Analytical Assumptions
- Back-to-back structures with matching pricing formulas effectively eliminate flat price risk, but basis, timing, and operational risks remain material
- Off-spec risk is increasing as US export terminal throughput rises and turnaround times compress
- Cross-contamination risk is manageable through proper tank cleaning protocols but cannot be fully eliminated
- Reliquefaction failure probability is low on modern vessels but consequences are severe (cargo loss, safety risk, contractual liability)
- The 0.5% manifest error allowance adequately covers measurement uncertainty, temperature effects, and boil-off for typical VLGC voyages
- Buyers under CFR terms face an inherent information asymmetry regarding vessel condition and transit risk since they bear risk but do not control the vessel

## Scenario Analysis

### Base Case
- Back-to-back contract structures continue to dominate, with traders earning $5-15/mt margins after freight and operational costs
- Off-spec events occur on 1-3% of cargoes, resolved through price discounts or blending at destination
- BOG losses remain within 0.1-0.3% of cargo volume per voyage on modern VLGCs
- Independent inspection costs are routine and absorbed as a standard operating cost ($50,000-100,000 per cargo)

### Bull Case
- Tight supply conditions create seller's market, allowing favorable quality tolerances and wider manifest error provisions
- Improved reliquefaction technology reduces BOG losses below 0.1%, improving cargo economics
- Standardized digital contract platforms reduce documentation errors and accelerate settlement
- New origin supply sources (e.g., Canadian West Coast, East Africa) create fresh back-to-back arbitrage structures with wider margins

### Bear Case
- Surge in off-spec cargoes from overloaded export terminals leads to widespread cargo rejections and price claims
- Reliquefaction failure on an aging VLGC fleet leads to multiple cargo loss events simultaneously
- Counterparty defaults on back-to-back structures leave traders with unhedged flat price exposure
- Tightening quality specifications at Asian import terminals increase rejection rates and require costly blending infrastructure

### Invalidation Triggers
- Shift from cargo-by-cargo contracting to long-term integrated supply agreements (reducing back-to-back trading opportunities)
- Blockchain-based B/L and smart contracts eliminating documentation risk and disputes entirely
- Asian buyers vertically integrating upstream (owning US export capacity) bypassing independent traders
- Standardization of LPG quality globally, eliminating origin-specific quality risks

## Downside Risks

- **Off-spec cargo rejection**: Buyer rejection of off-spec cargo at discharge port forces distressed resale or costly return voyage; linked to quality provisions in [[Physical LPG Contract Logic]]
- **Counterparty default in back-to-back**: If the buy-side or sell-side counterparty defaults, the trader faces unhedged flat price and freight exposure; see [[Basis Risk Management]]
- **Reliquefaction failure**: Cargo losses from BOG venting during transit reduce delivered quantity below contractual minimums, triggering claims
- **Measurement disputes**: Quantity differences between load and discharge exceeding manifest error tolerance result in financial claims; reconciliation process linked to [[Demurrage and Laytime]] SOF procedures
- **CFR transit risk mismatch**: Buyer bears transit risk without vessel control, creating exposure to vessel delays, diversions, and damage; see [[Incoterms in LPG Trading]] and [[VLGC Freight Dynamics]]
- **Title and financing risk**: Delayed or discrepant documentation can disrupt L/C payment flows, creating working capital strain and potential forced position liquidation
