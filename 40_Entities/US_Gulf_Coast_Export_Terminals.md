# US Gulf Coast Export Terminals

## Overview

The US Gulf Coast (USGC) hosts the **largest concentration of LPG export terminal infrastructure** in the world, enabling the United States to be the global leader in waterborne LPG exports. These facilities connect the prolific US shale production basins to international markets via VLGC loadings, with the [[Mont Belvieu]] hub serving as the inland pricing and logistics nexus.

## Key Facilities

- **Enterprise Products Partners** operates the largest LPG export terminal on the Houston Ship Channel, with multiple deep-water docks capable of simultaneously loading VLGCs. Enterprise's terminal is directly connected to Mont Belvieu storage and fractionation.
- **Targa Resources** operates LPG export capacity at Galena Park near Houston, handling propane, butane, and natural gasoline exports.
- **ONEOK** has expanded its Gulf Coast NGL infrastructure to include export-oriented facilities feeding international trade flows.
- **Marcus Hook** (near Philadelphia, Pennsylvania) serves as the primary **East Coast** LPG export terminal, connected to Marcellus/Utica shale production via the Mariner East pipeline system. Marcus Hook cargoes typically serve European and occasionally Asian markets.

## Operational Characteristics

- USGC terminals load VLGCs with standard cargo sizes of approximately **44,000 metric tonnes** of propane or butane.
- Loading rates, berth availability, and weather disruptions (particularly hurricane season from June to November) directly affect export reliability.
- Terminal operators offer both term and spot loading slots; term commitments typically carry volume-or-pay obligations.

## Market Connectivity

- USGC export volumes are the **marginal supply source** for global LPG markets, meaning incremental Asian or European demand is predominantly met by additional US cargoes.
- The FOB USGC price (anchored at [[Mont Belvieu]]) plus freight, canal tolls, and insurance determines the delivered cost to Asia, which competes against Middle East CP-priced cargoes.
- [[Geographical Arbitrage]] between USGC and Far East destinations is the central trade in global LPG markets.

## Growth Trajectory

- US LPG export capacity has expanded dramatically since 2015, driven by shale production growth and infrastructure investment.
- Continued capacity additions are planned through the late 2020s, reinforcing the USGC's role as the world's primary LPG export corridor.

## Associated Risks

- **Operational disruption risk**: Berth congestion, equipment failures, and terminal maintenance outages can delay VLGC loadings, cascading into cargo deferrals and delivery window breaches — see [[Physical LPG Contract Logic]]
- **Weather event risk**: Hurricane season (June-November) poses direct threat to terminal operations; storm shutdowns can halt loadings for days or weeks, creating supply gaps and freight dislocations
- **Tank-top risk**: Rapid inventory builds at terminals when export demand slows or vessel scheduling gaps emerge can force price concessions; conversely, low inventory risks loading delays — see [[Basis Risk Management]]
- **Demurrage exposure**: Vessel queue times at USGC berths during peak loading periods generate demurrage charges that erode cargo margins — see [[Demurrage and Laytime]]
- **Freight and routing risk**: Export economics depend on [[VLGC Freight Dynamics]] and transit availability through the [[Panama Canal]]; disruptions to either alter arbitrage viability
- **Volume-or-pay risk**: Term loading commitments carry volume-or-pay obligations; inability to load due to operational or weather events creates financial exposure under take-or-pay structures — see [[Physical Hedging Architecture]]
