### 1. Deal Structuring Logic
Experienced traders unbundle physical contracts into separate, tradable 
components to optimize their P&L:
*   **Freight Optionality:** Freight is not just a cost; it is a tradable 
variable that can open or close an arbitrage window [1]. By controlling the 
shipping, a trader can capture geographical arbitrage, such as executing 
triangular routes or exercising "diversion rights" to redirect a US cargo to 
Europe instead of Asia if the netback suddenly improves [2]. 
*   **Timing Risk:** Timing is aggressively managed around "crossover 
cargoes"—cargoes scheduled to load or discharge at the very end of one month or
the start of the next [3]. Traders must strictly define pricing periods or 
negotiate flat prices for these cargoes so the counterparty cannot deliberately
speed up or delay loading to capture paper market gains [3].
*   **Credit Exposure:** A single VLGC cargo represents massive financial 
exposure (e.g., $25 million *illustrative*) [4]. Traders structure deals using 
Letters of Credit (LCs) or Standby LCs (SBLC) to secure payment before loading 
[5]. They also utilize netting agreements to reduce gross credit exposure and 
daily margin calls to manage real-time mark-to-market (MtM) volatility [6].
*   **Benchmark Risk:** Traders must align their physical pricing with their 
paper hedges to avoid basis risk [7]. If you buy physical butane but hedge with
a propane swap, and the spread between the two blows out, your margin is 
destroyed [7]. To mitigate benchmark location risk, traders often negotiate 
"index-plus" pricing with suppliers rather than fixed posted prices [7].

### 2. FOB vs CFR Strategic Choice
The choice between FOB (Free On Board) and CFR (Cost and Freight) dictates who 
captures the logistics margin:
*   **When Traders Prefer FOB:** Large, independent trading houses (like Vitol 
or Gunvor) heavily prefer buying FOB because they manage their own VLGC fleets,
allowing them to optimize shipping routes and capture freight arbitrage [8]. 
Additionally, traditional Asian term buyers (especially in Japan and Korea) 
prefer FOB purchases using their own time-chartered VLGCs, giving them absolute
control over delivery scheduling and cost management [9-11].
*   **When Traders Prefer CFR:** Sellers prefer to sell CFR when they want to 
maintain control of the logistics to capture the embedded freight premium [8]. 
Conversely, CFR is highly preferred by buyers in more fragmented, 
price-sensitive spot markets (like China or Southeast Asia) who want to avoid 
the complexities and risks of managing international shipping logistics [8, 
12].

### 3. Cargo Optionality
Flexibility in a physical contract is a highly tradable asset:
*   **Laycan Flexibility:** Laycans (loading windows) dictate operational 
stress. Term contracts often provide consistent laycans to guarantee supply, 
whereas spot tenders offer delivery timing optionality but expose the buyer to 
market volatility [13]. In sophisticated forward markets, a seller might be 
required to give 15 days' notice for a highly specific 3-day loading window, 
leaving almost no room for operational error [14]. 
*   **Delivery Windows and Volume Optionality:** Contracts frequently include 
volume optionality, allowing counterparties to adjust contracted volumes to 
optimize supply [15]. However, this optionality is notoriously difficult to 
value mathematically because it lacks a single strike price and involves 
shifting volumes across different pricing periods [16]. 

### 4. Structuring Trades with Refinery Supply
For a refinery-linked desk, the primary directive is preventing a refinery 
shutdown:
*   **Tank-Top Mitigation:** LPG storage requires highly expensive specialized 
steel, so refinery tanks are typically built to "just meet" offtake parcel 
sizes [17, 18]. If a trader fails to schedule a vessel on time, the refinery 
faces "tank-tops" (full storage) and must catastrophically shut in upstream oil
and gas production [18, 19].
*   **Aggressive Cargo Placement:** To avoid tank-tops, refineries without 
export alternatives or vast storage must become aggressive marketers. They will
discount cargoes to clear inventory, meaning the desk must prioritize swift, 
reliable placement over holding out for the absolute highest spot price [20].
*   **Parcel Size Optimization:** Because of strict storage limitations, 
Australian and Southeast Asian producers often cannot load a full 44,000 mt of 
a single grade (e.g., butane) onto a VLGC [21, 22]. Traders must creatively 
structure "co-loaded" cargoes (e.g., 1 compartment of propane, 3 of butane) to 
avoid paying deadfreight penalties [21].

### 5. Hidden Risks in Deal Structures
Even a perfectly hedged trade can bleed out through structural blind spots:
*   **Quality/Spec Rejections:** A cargo must meet rigorous specifications. If 
a trader delivers an "off-spec" cargo (e.g., containing methanol or high 
sulfur), high-precision buyers like PDH plants will reject it entirely, 
destroying the trade's value [5].
*   **Operational Demurrage and Cooling Failures:** A delayed vessel waiting 
for a berth can incur demurrage costs of $50,000+ per day (*illustrative*) [4].
Furthermore, if the vessel's reliquefaction plant fails and the refrigerated 
cargo warms up, discharge terminals will reject the ship, resulting in 
catastrophic delays [4].
*   **Conversion Factor Discrepancies:** (Drawn from parallel LNG market 
dynamics): Complex pricing formulas often mix energy units (MMBtu) with 
volumetric units (Metric Tons). Because every cargo has a slightly different 
energy density, standardizing these conversion factors is impossible, leading 
to accumulating rounding errors and pricing disputes that erode P&L [23].
