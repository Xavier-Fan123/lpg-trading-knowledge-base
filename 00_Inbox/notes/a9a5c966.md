### 1) Core Market Logic
In physical LPG trading, the contract is the ultimate risk management tool. 
Operational risks—vessel delays, weather disruptions, and mechanical 
failures—are inevitable. The contract dictates *who pays* when things go wrong.

Because LPG requires specialized, high-cost infrastructure (cryogenic steel 
tanks and refrigerated vessels), logistics are much less flexible than in 
standard crude oil markets [3]. When executing a trade, especially back-to-back
structures (buying FOB to sell CFR), any misalignment in title transfer, risk 
transfer, or inspection clauses creates a "basis risk" in your contract. If the
buyer's terms don't perfectly mirror the seller's terms, our desk absorbs the 
financial impact of any physical dispute.

### 2) Key Concepts
*   **Demurrage & Tank-Tops:** Demurrage is the penalty paid for delaying a 
vessel beyond its allowed laytime, often costing $50,000 or more per day [4]. 
Conversely, if a ship arrives late to lift our Brunei production, we face 
"tank-top" risk, where storage fills to capacity, necessitating the suspension 
of upstream production [1, 2].
*   **Risk vs. Title Transfer:** Title (ownership) and Risk (liability for 
loss/damage) do not always transfer at the same time. In FOB and CFR 
deliveries, risk typically transfers from the seller to the buyer the moment 
the goods pass the vessel's permanent hose connection at the loading terminal 
[5, 6]. 
*   **The "Manifest Error or Fraud" Exception:** The quantity and quality of 
the cargo are determined by an independent inspector (like SGS or Alex Stewart)
at the loading terminal [7, 8]. Their certificate is conclusive and binding for
invoicing purposes *except* in cases of "manifest error or fraud" [9].
*   **Off-Spec Contamination:** LPG requires high-precision specifications. 
High levels of contaminants, specifically methanol, pose severe operational 
risks to petrochemical buyers (like PDH plants), leading to potential cargo 
rejection [8, 10].
*   **Reliquefaction Failure:** Refrigerated LPG must be kept extremely cold 
(e.g., -45°C for propane) [11]. If a vessel's reliquefaction plant fails, the 
cargo warms and expands, making it impossible to discharge at certain terminals
without massive delays [4].

### 3) How Traders Actually Use This
Traders use contract clauses to aggressively defend their calculated margins. 

When structuring a back-to-back trade, you must ensure your purchase contract 
and sales contract are perfectly mirrored. If we buy LPG from a third party FOB
and sell it CFR to a Chinese buyer, we must stipulate that the independent 
inspector's results at the *load port* are binding for *both* contracts [7, 9].
If you agree to load port inspection for your purchase, but allow discharge 
port inspection for your sale, you have created a massive gap. Any volume lost 
to boil-off during the voyage, or any minor quality degradation, will result in
a short-payment from your buyer that you cannot claim back from your supplier.

For Hengyi's equity production, we use CIF or CFR terms to maintain control of 
the vessel logistics [16]. This allows us to optimize the ship's speed to 
manage our refinery's tank inventories perfectly, avoiding shut-ins [1].

### 4) Step-by-Step Trading Example
Let’s look at a back-to-back operational "gotcha."
*   **The Setup:** You buy 44,000 mt of LPG FOB Middle East and immediately 
sell it CFR South China. Your expected net margin is $15/mt *(Illustrative)*.
*   **The Execution:** The vessel loads smoothly. The independent inspector 
issues a clean certificate of quality at the load port. Risk transfers to the 
Chinese buyer as the LPG passes the loading hose [5, 6].
*   **The Event:** Mid-voyage, the vessel's reliquefaction plant suffers a 
partial failure, causing the cargo to warm up slightly [4]. 
*   **The Dispute:** The Chinese terminal cannot receive warm cargo and delays 
discharge. The buyer tries to pass the demurrage and quality claim back to you.
*   **The Resolution:** Because you sold CFR, the risk of transit transferred 
at the load port [6]. The binding inspection certificate proves the product was
on-spec when it crossed the ship's rail [9]. Legally, the buyer must pay your 
invoice in full and pursue their own marine insurance or the shipowner for the 
loss. Your margin is protected by the contract.

### 5) Trader Decision Process
**Go:**
*   Independent inspection clauses are identical on both sides of a 
back-to-back trade.
*   The nominated vessel is fully vetted and cleared by both the load port and 
the discharge port authorities [8].
*   Laydays (the loading window) have sufficient buffer built in to prevent 
refinery tank-tops in case the vessel is delayed by weather.

**No-Go (Trade Killers):**
*   **Quality Mismatch:** The buyer demands a strict methanol limit that our 
Brunei refinery or our FOB supplier cannot guarantee [10].
*   **Credit/Documentation Snags:** The buyer requests a Discharge Port 
quantity settlement (Outturn basis) [17] instead of Load Port, exposing us to 
volume shrinkage risk without adequate price compensation.
*   **Logistics Congestion:** Severe fog in the US Gulf Coast or heavy delays 
at the Panama Canal threaten to blow past our contractual delivery dates [18, 
19].

### 6) Common Trader Mistakes
*   **Misunderstanding CFR Risk Transfer:** Traders often assume that because 
they are paying for freight in a CFR deal, they own the risk until the ship 
arrives. Risk transfers at the *load port* [6]. If the ship sinks, the buyer 
still owes you the money (which is why buyers often prefer CIF so the seller 
handles the insurance) [16].
*   **Ignoring the "Manifest Error" Clause:** Assuming an inspection 
certificate is absolute. If a buyer can prove the inspector's equipment was 
faulty or calibrated incorrectly, they can invoke "manifest error" and claim 
against you [9, 20].
*   **Sloppy Vessel Vetting:** Fixing a cheap vessel that hasn't been approved 
by the discharge terminal. If the terminal rejects the ship, you are stuck with
a loaded floating asset bleeding $50,000/day in demurrage [4, 8].

### 8) Answer Framework
1.  **Assert the Contract Term:** State clearly where risk and title 
transferred based on the CFR Incoterm [5, 6].
2.  **Invoke the Inspection Clause:** Remind the buyer that the load port 
independent inspector's certificate is conclusive and binding for invoicing 
purposes [9, 20].
3.  **Identify the Burden of Proof:** State the specific legal exception (the 
two specific conditions) the buyer must legally prove to challenge the load 
port certificate [9].
4.  **Commercial Mitigation:** Decide whether to strictly enforce the LC 
drawing or commercialize a solution to prevent the ship from being stranded and
accruing demurrage [4].

### 9) Real-World Signals to Monitor

*   **Regional Quality Alerts:** Market chatter about off-spec or high-methanol
cargoes circulating in the spot market [10].
