**STRATEGIC BRIEF 03: Demurrage Mechanics, Laytime Calculus, and SOF 
Reconciliation**

To effectively capture and attribute logistics-driven P&L erosion, your desk 
must treat demurrage not as an operational afterthought, but as a continuous 
financial derivative—specifically, a penalty function modeled as liquidated 
damages for breach of the charterparty's time allowance [1].

### I. The Standard Demurrage Liability Formula
Within Endur’s cMotion/gMotion logistics modules [2], the standard algorithmic 
definition for Demurrage Liability ($\Pi_{Dem}$) is computed dynamically as a 
piecewise function. It triggers the moment Used Laytime ($\Sigma T_{used}$) 
exceeds Allowed Laytime ($L_{allowed}$):

$$\Pi_{Dem} = \max\left[0, \left( (T_{comp} - T_{comm}) - \sum T_{exc} \right) 
- L_{allowed} \right] \times \frac{R_D}{24}$$

Where:
*   $T_{comp}$ / $T_{comm}$: The timestamps for completion and commencement of 
laytime (derived from the Statement of Facts).
*   $\sum T_{exc}$: The sum of all contractual laytime exceptions (e.g., Free 
Pratique, deballasting, concurrent stripping) [3].
*   $L_{allowed}$: Allowed laytime, standardized at 24 hours for standard LPG 
deliveries [3].
*   $R_D$: The daily demurrage rate (e.g., prevailing $50,000/day for VLGCs) 
[4]. 

### II. Complex SOF Scenario: NOR, Congestion, and the Weather Delay
Let’s break down the exact financial routing of the provided SOF scenario:
1.  **NOR Tendered at Anchorage:** Laytime commencement is structurally 
hardcoded. Under standard Shell or ASBATANKVOY terms, the laytime clock starts 
either upon berthing or **NOR + 6 hours**, whichever is earlier [3]. 
2.  **48-Hour Terminal Congestion:** Under standard WIBON (Whether In Berth Or 
Not) clauses, "Time Lost Waiting for Berth" shifts the risk of congestion 
entirely onto the charterer/receiver [1]. If the vessel waits 48 hours, the 
standard 24-hour LPG laytime allowance is completely exhausted while at 
anchorage. 
3.  **12-Hour Weather Delay (Rain):** Standard laytime often includes weather 
exceptions like WWD (Weather Working Days) or SHEX (Sundays & Holidays 
Excepted) [1]. *However*, because the 48-hour congestion already exhausted the 
24 hours of $L_{allowed}$, the vessel went on demurrage at Hour 30 (NOR+6 + 
24). 
4.  **The Financial Impact:** The ironclad maritime law maxim applies: **"Once 
on Demurrage, Always on Demurrage"** [1]. Because the vessel is already on 
demurrage when the rain starts, the weather exception becomes null and void. 
The 12 hours of rain accrue demurrage continuously at 100% of the $R_D$ rate, 
adding an immediate -$25,000 P&L hit (assuming a $50k/day VLGC rate).

### III. FOB vs. CFR Contractual Treatment of Laytime
The geographical benchmark defines who holds the unhedged exposure to the "Time
Lost Waiting for Berth":
*   **FOB (Free On Board):** The Buyer charters the vessel and holds the 
freight risk. However, if the Seller’s terminal is congested, the Buyer’s 
operations team will pass the Demurrage claim back to the Seller. The SPA 
(Sales & Purchase Agreement) must strictly mirror the charterparty laytime 
clauses to prevent basis bleed where the Buyer pays the shipowner but cannot 
recover from the Seller. 
*   **CFR (Cost & Freight):** The Seller controls the vessel and freight. Risk 
and title transfer at the load port, but the Buyer is liable for discharge 
delays [3]. Under CFR, "Time Lost Waiting for Berth" at the destination port 
hits the Buyer's P&L directly, as the Seller's shipowner will levy the 
demurrage against the Seller, who passes it strictly to the CFR Buyer.

### IV. Mathematical Deduction for Pumping Warranty Breaches
The standard LPG delivery warranty dictates that the vessel must be capable of 
discharging its full cargo within 24 hours, **or** maintain 100 PSI at the 
vessel’s manifold, *provided shore facilities permit* [3]. 

If the receiving terminal breaches this setup—meaning the shore cannot safely 
accept the cargo at the warranted rate, or applies back-pressure exceeding 100 
PSI—the vessel is exonerated, and the excess time counts fully as Demurrage 
against the terminal/Buyer. 

Conversely, if the terminal *is* capable, but the **vessel** breaches its 
pumping warranty (e.g., discharge takes 36 hours due to weak pumps), the exact 
mathematical deduction applied against the Shipowner’s demurrage claim is:

$$\Delta T_{penalty} = T_{actual} - \max\left(24, 
\frac{V_{cargo}}{Rate_{shore\_limit}}\right)$$
$$Financial\ Deduction = \Delta T_{penalty} \times \left( \frac{R_D}{24} 
\right)$$

If the cargo is a Part Cargo Lot, the 24-hour allowance is pro-rated 
mathematically: 
$L_{allowed} = 24 \times \left( \frac{Volume_{PartCargo} + 5\%}{Total\ 
Volume_{Loaded}} \right)$ [3].
