### 1) Core Market Logic
The global LPG market has fundamentally changed from a regional byproduct 
market into a globalized commodity, driven heavily by petrochemical 
industrialization in Asia [1]. China is the undisputed gravitational center of 
the LPG world, consuming over 96 million tonnes annually, and its market is 
primarily driven by Propane Dehydrogenation (PDH) plants [2]. 

Unlike flexible steam crackers (which can switch between naphtha and LPG), PDH 
plants utilize propane as a *dedicated* feedstock to produce propylene [2]. 
Because they cannot switch feedstocks, their only defense against high propane 
prices is to shut down or reduce operating rates [3, 4]. Therefore, Chinese PDH
plants represent the "swing demand" that professional traders watch most 
closely [2]. If their margins are positive, they buy spot cargoes aggressively,
driving up the Argus Far East Index (AFEI) [5]. If their margins bleed, they 
cut runs, destroying global marginal demand and crashing the AFEI [4].

### 2) Key Concepts
*   **The PDH Margin Formula:** Traders build crack spread models to anticipate
demand using this exact logic: $PDH\ Margin = Price_{Propylene} - 
Price_{Propane} - OpEx$ [4].
*   **The "Two-Week" Pain Threshold:** Run-rate cuts are not instantaneous. If 
the PDH margin stays "in the red" (negative) for more than two weeks, PDH 
plants will cut run rates to 60-65% [4].
*   **Run-Rate Signals:** High PDH run rates (above 75%) signal strong, active 
propane demand [6]. For example, when margins are supported by strong Chinese 
polypropylene futures, manufacturers lock in profits and keep run rates steady 
[7]. 
*   **Spot vs. Term Shift:** Chinese PDH buyers have increasingly shifted away 
from long-term supply contracts (like Saudi CP term deals) toward spot market 
buying (CFR and FOB) [8, 9]. They do this to avoid being locked into inflated 
term prices during volatile periods, making their spot tender activity a 
massive driver of prompt AFEI pricing [8, 9].
*   **Contaminant Sensitivity:** PDH operations require high-precision 
feedstock. Cargoes containing high levels of methanol contamination pose severe
operational risks to PDH units, rendering "off-spec" cargoes highly problematic
to place in China [10, 11].
*   **Downstream Oversupply Threat:** Even if propane feedstock costs fall, PDH
margins can still be crushed if there is an oversupply in downstream markets 
(like new polypropylene capacity coming online), which depresses the value of 
the propylene output [12].

### 3) How Traders Actually Use This
Traders use PDH margin calculations to predict physical spot demand *before* 
the buyers actually enter the market. 

If you see Dalian Polypropylene (PP) futures rising, you know PDH margins are 
improving [6, 7]. As a trader, you know Chinese PDH operators will want to lock
in these profits, meaning a wave of spot tenders is coming. You immediately get
long on AFEI paper or hold onto your physical FOB cargoes, anticipating you can
sell them CFR China at a premium [4, 13]. 

Conversely, if the PDH margin turns negative, the trader knows that within two 
weeks, Chinese plants will slash run rates to 60-65% [4]. At our Hengyi desk, 
if we are generating a natural long physical position of propane from our 
Brunei refinery, a negative PDH margin is a glaring red warning. We must 
immediately look to short the Far East propane paper market to hedge our 
physical exposure [4], or urgently redirect our physical cargoes to buyers in 
price-inelastic markets (like India's residential sector) before the Asian spot
market collapses [14].

### 4) Step-by-Step Trading Example
Let's look at a historical margin squeeze from late 2023.
*   **The Setup:** In August 2023, Chinese PDH plant run rates hit a peak of 
about 85% [15].
*   **The Shock:** By September, the average Far East propane price surged 10% 
to roughly $695/mt, while downstream propylene and polypropylene prices only 
rose 4% [3]. 
*   **The Margin Calculation:** Using the formula ($Price_{Propylene} - 
Price_{Propane} - OpEx$), the rapid rise in the propane input cost completely 
overwhelmed the slight rise in the propylene output price, crushing the margin 
[3, 4].
*   **The Market Action:** Because the margin went deep into the red, Chinese 
PDH operators systematically slashed their plant run rates. By the third week 
of September, average run rates had plummeted from 85% down to 65% [15]. 
*   **The P&L Impact:** A trader tracking this margin compression in early 
September would have aggressively shorted AFEI swaps, profiting massively as 
the physical spot demand from China evaporated over the following weeks [4].

### 5) Trader Decision Process
**Go:**
*   PDH Margin is solidly positive.
*   Dalian PP futures are trending upward [6].
*   PDH run rates are reported above 75% by data providers like OilChem or 
Argus [6].

**No-Go (Trade Killers):**
*   **Margin "In the Red" for > 14 Days:** If margins have been negative for 
two weeks, immediate demand destruction is imminent [4].
*   **Off-Spec Molecule:** If our Brunei physical cargo tests high for methanol
or moisture, Chinese PDH buyers will reject it, destroying your CFR China 
arbitrage strategy [10, 11].
*   **Downstream Supply Glut:** If multiple new Chinese polypropylene plants 
are scheduled to come online in the same quarter, downstream prices will likely
crash, destroying PDH margins regardless of what the AFEI is doing [12].

### 6) Common Trader Mistakes
*   **Treating Chinese Demand as Inelastic:** Assuming China will always buy 
propane. Unlike India, which buys term volumes steadily for residential cooking
(highly inelastic), China's PDH demand is purely margin-driven and highly 
opportunistic [14]. If the math doesn't work, they stop buying.
*   **Ignoring the Time Lag:** Panicking and shorting the market on day one of 
negative margins. Traders must remember the two-week pain threshold before run 
rates are actually cut to 60-65% [4]. 
*   **Focusing Only on the Feedstock Leg:** Staring obsessively at the AFEI 
price but ignoring the Dalian PP futures. The absolute price of propane doesn't
matter to a PDH plant; only the *spread* between propane and propylene matters 
[4].
