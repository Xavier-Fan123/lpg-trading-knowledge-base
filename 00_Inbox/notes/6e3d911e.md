Title: Commercial Operations and Physical Pricing Mechanisms
Content:
Welcome back to the desk. This module is where the physical reality of moving a
cargo collides directly with your financial P&L. A perfectly modeled trade is 
useless if your Operations (Ops) team and Middle Office are not in perfect sync
regarding when a cargo loads, when it prices, and what its exact chemical 
composition is.

For our Hengyi desk, managing the export of Brunei equity production, the 
Commercial Ops team is our last line of defense. If a vessel is delayed, the 
physical pricing window shifts, and the trader must immediately adjust the 
financial hedges to prevent a massive basis risk blowout.

Here is the deep dive into Commercial Ops and Pricing Mechanisms.

***

### 1) Core Market Logic
In physical LPG trading, the final invoice price is almost never known on the 
day the deal is signed. Instead, the price is "floating" and tethered to 
specific operational triggers—most commonly the Bill of Lading (B/L) date at 
the load port or the Notice of Readiness (NOR) date at the discharge port. 

Because of this, the operational timeline *is* the pricing mechanism. If bad 
weather or terminal congestion delays a vessel, the physical pricing window 
automatically shifts to a different set of days. If the trader’s paper hedges 
are not dynamically "rolled" by the Middle Office to match this new operational
reality, the desk's margin is exposed to unhedged market volatility.

### 2) Key Concepts
*   **Ops Responsibilities (FOB vs. CFR/DES):** 
    *   *FOB (Free On Board):* The buyer's Ops team must charter the vessel, 
ensure it passes terminal vetting (e.g., SIRE inspections), and manage the 
voyage. Risk transfers to the buyer the moment the goods are loaded [1, 2].
    *   *CFR (Cost and Freight):* The seller's Ops team handles the vessel 
chartering and freight logistics, though the buyer still holds the insurance 
and transit risk [3]. 
    *   *DES (Delivery Ex-Ship):* Used for specific destination pricing [4]. 
*(Note: The exact operational risk transfer mechanism for DES is **not found in
sources**, but traders know it generally means the seller bears all risks and 
costs until the ship arrives at the destination).*
*   **Pricing Window (B/L Date vs. NOR Date):** 
    *   *B/L Date:* The Bill of Lading serves as the receipt of loaded cargo 
[5]. In many spot transactions, the B/L date anchors the pricing formula (e.g.,
pricing based on the average of published quotes 5 days before and 5 days after
the B/L date) [5]. 
    *   *NOR Date:* The Notice of Readiness confirms the vessel has arrived and
is ready to discharge [6]. For some CFR deliveries, the NOR date triggers the 
payment window (e.g., payment due within 7 days of NOR) [7].
*   **The "Crossover" Cargo Risk:** Cargoes scheduled to load exactly at the 
end of one month or the start of the next. A delay of just 24 hours can shift 
the B/L date into a new month, fundamentally changing the underlying index 
price used for settlement [8].
*   **Independent Surveyor Role:** Agencies like SGS or Alex Stewart are hired 
to conduct pre-shipment quality and quantity surveys [2]. They test the LPG to 
ensure it meets strict specifications, such as a maximum sulfur content of 
30ppm or 50ppm (using ASTM D-3246), vapor pressure limits, and correct 
propane/butane ratios [2, 9, 10].
*   **The Claims Process:** If there is a quantity variation or quality 
failure, the Boarding Officer must immediately issue a "protest letter" to the 
ship's Master [11]. Formal claims must then be submitted with full supporting 
documentation within strict contractual time limits (e.g., 90 days under 
standard Shell GT&Cs) [12].

### 3) How Traders Actually Use This
Traders rely on a continuous feedback loop between the Ops team and the Middle 
Office to manage exposure. 

The Ops team maintains a daily cargo tracking system that monitors vessel 
itineraries, B/L dates, and ETAs [13]. If Ops sees that our chartered VLGC 
loading in Brunei is delayed by a typhoon, they immediately notify the Middle 
Office and the trader. 

Because the B/L date will now fall into the next calendar month, the physical 
cargo will price against next month's index. The trader must execute a 
"roll"—buying back the current month's paper hedge and selling the next month's
paper hedge—to realign the financial protection with the new physical delivery 
window [14]. 

### 4) Step-by-Step Trading Example
Let’s look at how a B/L date slip impacts pricing and requires Ops/Trader 
coordination.
*   **The Setup:** We sell 22,000 mt of Brunei propane FOB to a buyer, priced 
at the average of the *Month of B/L* Saudi CP. The laycan is October 29-31.
*   **The Hedge:** The trader shorts 22,000 mt of October CP swaps to lock in 
the margin.
*   **The Delay:** The buyer's vessel fails its initial vetting and is delayed,
finally arriving and completing loading on November 1. The B/L is dated 
November 1.
*   **The Pricing Shift:** Because the B/L is now in November, the buyer will 
pay us based on the *November* Saudi CP, not the October CP. 
*   **The Action:** Ops immediately alerts the trader on October 30 that the 
ship will miss the October window. The trader quickly buys back the October 
swaps and shorts November swaps. By communicating early, the desk successfully 
rolls the hedge, avoiding a catastrophic basis blowout between October and 
November prices.

### 5) Trader Decision Process
**Go:**
*   Surveyor (SGS) issues a clean Certificate of Quality at the load port 
confirming the exact required propane/butane ratio and sulfur limits [2].
*   Ops confirms the B/L date perfectly matches the pricing month we have 
hedged on paper.
*   The Letter of Credit (LC) validity dates provide enough buffer for minor 
weather delays.

**No-Go (Trade Killers):**
*   **Off-Spec Molecule:** The surveyor detects high moisture or sulfur. 
High-precision petrochemical buyers (like PDH plants) will reject the cargo 
outright upon discharge [15].
*   **NOR Mismatch:** The buyer demands pricing based on the discharge port NOR
date, but our upstream supplier prices us based on the load port B/L date, 
creating an unhedgeable timing basis risk.
*   **Missed Claim Window:** Ops fails to file a formal claim with full 
documentation within the 90-day window for a cargo that arrived short on 
volume, automatically forfeiting the desk's right to compensation [12].

### 6) Common Trader Mistakes
*   **Siloed Departments:** Traders booking a deal and ignoring Ops. If Ops 
doesn't feed real-time B/L date changes to the Middle Office for exposure 
reconciliation [13], the desk ends up holding "naked" (unbacked) paper 
positions.
*   **Ignoring Conversion Factors in Settlement:** Because Asian LNG/LPG 
formulas often mix energy units with volumetric units, relying on generic 
conversion factors instead of the surveyor's exact cargo-specific density can 
lead to accumulating rounding errors and lost margin [16].
*   **Sloppy Letter of Protest:** Failing to have the Boarding Officer 
immediately issue a protest letter to the Master when the discharged volume 
does not match the loaded volume [11], severely weakening the commercial claim 
later.

*   **The Quality Issue:** During loading, the independent surveyor notes a

**Task:** 
1. What specific financial action must you (the trader) take today regarding 
your September swaps to protect your P&L? 
2. How do you commercially defend against the buyer's threat of rejection using

### 8) Answer Framework
1.  **Identify the Pricing Shift:** State which month the physical cargo will 
now price against based on the new B/L date.
2.  **Execute the Roll:** Detail the exact paper trades (buy/sell) required to 
move your hedge from the old month to the new month.
3.  **Address the Quality Dispute:** Reference the standard maximum sulfur 
specification (ppm) and the binding nature of the load port surveyor's 
certificate to enforce the buyer's obligation to take delivery.

### 9) Real-World Signals to Monitor
*   **Daily Vessel Tracking:** Monitored by Ops to predict if a vessel will 
cross over into a new calendar month before the B/L is issued [13].
*   **Surveyor Pre-Shipment Reports:** Reviewed immediately for sulfur, 
olefins, and propane/butane ratios before the ship departs [10].
*   **Statement of Facts (SOF):** Countersigned logs detailing every minute of 
loading/discharge, critical for filing demurrage claims [17].
