### 1. Arbitrage Microstructure
Advanced arbitrage is less about absolute price direction and entirely about 
exploiting microstructure inefficiencies between the physical cargo and the 
financial paper used to hedge it.

*   **Cargo Timing vs Index Windows:** The Argus Far East Index (AFEI) assesses
deliveries 25-40 days forward [1, 2]. However, physical delivery windows rarely
match this perfectly. If you are trading a "crossover cargo"—a cargo scheduled 
to load/discharge precisely at the end of one month or the start of the 
next—buyers and sellers will attempt to speed up or delay loadings to optimize 
against the pricing month [3, 4]. Traders must negotiate "flat prices" or 
strictly defined pricing periods for these crossover cargoes so the 
counterparty cannot manipulate the loading date for paper gains [3, 4].
*   **Laycan Optionality:** Physical laycans (loading windows) are typically 
5-20 days [5]. The optionality of *when* a vessel arrives within that window is
a highly tradable asset. If a market is in steep backwardation, arriving on day
1 of the laycan versus day 15 changes the cargo's value massively.
*   **Freight Fixing Timing:** A senior trader does not always fix freight at 
the exact moment they buy the FOB cargo. If they anticipate a drop in VLGC 
rates, they may hold the physical cargo "naked" on freight. Conversely, during 
geopolitical tension, traders will fix vessels much further out in time than 
normal to secure capacity, which paradoxically creates a shortage of available 
ships and pushes spot rates up before the cargo even loads [6].
*   **Price Discovery Between Sellers and Buyers:** The Asian spot market is 
surprisingly illiquid in terms of true "flat price" deals; typically, only 1-2 
full cargoes trade on a fixed flat price per week [3, 7, 8]. Most deals are 
index-linked. Therefore, price discovery relies heavily on the Platts Market on
Close (MOC) process, where bids and offers are tested in real-time increments 
(e.g., 5 cents/MMBtu every 20 seconds for derivatives) [9, 10]. Senior traders 
use part-parcel trades to test or influence these daily assessments [11, 12].

### 2. Multi-Region Arbitrage Framework
A senior trader evaluates the globe simultaneously. The US Gulf Coast (USGC) 
acts as the global marginal supplier and "arb setter," while the Middle East 
(ME) acts as the traditional term base [13]. For our Hengyi desk, Southeast 
Asia (SEA) is our structural advantage.

**The Decision Tree:**
1.  **Evaluate the Global Anchor (USGC → Asia):** Calculate the Mont Belvieu to
AFEI spread. Deduct USGC terminal fees, VLGC freight, and Panama Canal costs 
[14]. Is the net margin > $0? 
    *   *If Yes:* The "arb is open." Expect a flood of US VLGCs heading to 
Asia, which will eventually depress the AFEI and absorb global shipping 
capacity [15, 16].
    *   *If No:* US cargoes are trapped. Asia must rely on ME/SEA. 
2.  **Evaluate the Baseline (ME → Asia):** Compare the Saudi CP (plus 
ME-to-Asia freight) against the AFEI [17]. In 2025, the Saudi CP+ premium to 
AFEI widened to an extreme $83/mt due to supply constraints [18]. 
    *   *Action:* If CP is pricing at a massive premium to spot AFEI, avoid ME 
term liftings if possible and buy spot.
3.  **Optimize the Equity Play (SEA → China):** Evaluate Hengyi's Brunei 
production. Because our voyage time to China is a fraction of the US/ME routes,
our freight cost is significantly lower [19-21]. 
    *   *Action:* If global freight rates are skyrocketing (closing the US and 
ME arbs), the delivered CFR China price remains high. We capture this entire 
freight premium by selling our Brunei molecules CFR China on our own short-haul
vessels.

### 3. Sensitivity Analysis
A spreadsheet netback is useless if it breaks under real-world stress. Senior 
traders stress-test their models daily against:

*   **Freight Volatility & Routing:** The Panama Canal is the critical stress 
point. If drought reduces draft limits or locks, traders must model the cost of
auction slots versus the cost of rerouting via the Cape of Good Hope (COGH). 
Rerouting via COGH adds 10 to 15 days to the voyage [22]. This ties up capital,
extends the hedge window, and drastically increases bunker fuel costs. 
*   **Port Delays & Tank-Tops:** If a vessel is delayed arriving at a load port
(like our Brunei facility), the refinery faces "tank-top" risk. LPG storage is 
built to just meet offtake parcel sizes due to the high cost of cryogenic steel
[23, 24]. If the tanks fill, the refinery must shut in upstream oil and gas 
production [24, 25]. Traders must stress-test demurrage risk ($50,000+/day) 
[26] versus the catastrophic cost of a refinery shut-in.
*   **Benchmark Price Moves:** Traders stress-test the basis risk between CP 
and AFEI. CP is a lagging indicator (set monthly), while AFEI is a leading, 
spot-responsive indicator [27, 28]. If the market crashes mid-month, a trader 
holding CP-priced physical inventory hedged with AFEI swaps faces a massive 
basis blowout [18, 29]. 
*   **Quality Spreads:** You cannot just model "LPG." Traders must test the 
Propane-Naphtha (Pro-Nap) spread. If the discount of propane to naphtha shrinks
(e.g., from -$120/mt to -$38/mt), flexible petchem crackers will switch back to
naphtha, instantly destroying your projected Asian LPG demand [30-32]. 
Furthermore, discharge terminals often have strict limitations (e.g., only able
to take 11,000 mt of propane due to tankage limits), meaning full 44,000 mt 
single-grade cargoes face severe placement constraints [33, 34].

### 4. Second-Order Market Effects
Arbitrage isn't just reacting to prices; the flow of the physical commodity 
*creates* the next price movement. 
When the US-to-Asia arbitrage opens wide, traders charter VLGCs to move the 
volume. Because this is the longest trade route in the market, it creates 
massive "tonne-mile inflation" [15]. This absorbs the global supply of ships, 
causing VLGC spot freight rates to skyrocket [15, 35]. 

The second-order effect is that these extreme freight rates then automatically 
slam the US-to-Asia arbitrage shut, trapping US LPG in the Gulf Coast (crashing
Mont Belvieu prices) and starving Asia of spot cargoes (spiking the AFEI) [36, 
37]. For a regional producer like Hengyi, this is the optimal environment: 
global prices are high, but the global fleet is tied up, allowing us to command
massive premiums for our short-haul Brunei molecules.

### 5. Advanced Trading Exercise
You are managing the Hengyi SEA-to-North Asia book. It is late October.
*   **Target:** A Chinese PDH plant requires 44,000 mt of Propane delivered CFR
South China in late November.
*   **Current Benchmarks:** AFEI Propane is $650/mt. Mont Belvieu is $400/mt. 
Saudi CP is $610/mt. *(All prices illustrative).*
*   **Option A (USGC):** You can buy FOB Houston. Freight via Panama is 
normally $100/mt. However, the Canal just restricted transits. You must either 
pay a $400,000 auction fee to transit on time, or reroute via COGH, which adds 
14 days and $45/mt in fuel/charter costs, causing you to miss the buyer's 
laycan and face a $50,000/day penalty for late delivery. 
*   **Option B (ME):** You can buy FOB Ras Tanura at CP + $10/mt. Freight to 
China is $50/mt. 
*   **Option C (Hengyi Brunei):** We have 22,000 mt of Propane in tank. You 
would need to source the remaining 22,000 mt from the spot market (AFEI + 
$5/mt) to fill the VLGC. Short-haul freight is $25/mt.

**Task:** Calculate the delivered cost per tonne for all three options (assume 
the 44,000 mt baseline for calculations). Determine which route provides the 
optimal netback. How does the quality constraint of the Chinese buyer (PDH 
plant) impact your sourcing?

### 6. Decision Process
A senior trader evaluates this by moving beyond the base math:
1.  **Calculate the True US Netback:** The trader immediately discards the 
"normal" $100/mt US freight. They calculate the auction cost per tonne ($400k /
44k mt = ~$9/mt) vs. the COGH penalty (14 days late * $50k = $700k penalty + 
$45/mt extra freight). 
2.  **Evaluate Basis Risk on the ME Leg:** Buying CP to sell AFEI introduces 
timing basis risk. Since it is late October, the November CP hasn't been 
officially set yet. The trader must decide if they are willing to take the CP 
exposure or if they must execute a CP/AFEI paper swap to lock the margin.
3.  **Assess the Downstream Constraint:** The prompt notes the buyer is a *PDH 
plant*. PDH plants require high-purity propane and are highly sensitive to 
contaminants (like methanol) [38]. The trader must verify that the 22,000 mt 
sourced from the spot market for Option C perfectly matches Hengyi's quality 
specs, otherwise the entire 44,000 mt commingled cargo could be rejected upon 
discharge. 
4.  **The Final Call:** The trader executes the route with the highest 
risk-adjusted netback, ensuring that the paper hedges (Mont Belvieu, CP, or 
AFEI swaps) are layered in at the exact moment the physical FOB vessel is 
fixed.
