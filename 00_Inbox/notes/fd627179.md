To effectively trade the Asian marginal demand curve, your algorithms must 
dynamically price the substitution optionality embedded in flexible Northeast

### I. The Quantitative Switching Thresholds & Operational Leverage
The baseline switching logic is dictated by the Propane-Naphtha (Pro-Nap) 
spread. Standard market heuristics dictate two primary triggers for a switch 
from Naphtha to LPG:
1.  **The Absolute Spread:** $P_{Naphtha} - P_{Propane} \ge \$50/mt$ [1, 2].
2.  **The Price Ratio (90% Rule):** $\frac{P_{Propane}}{P_{Naphtha}} \le 0.90$ 
[2, 3].

However, relying purely on the flat feedstock cost differential ignores the 
**Operational Leverage Multiplier**. Because cracking lighter feedstocks 
requires lower vaporization energy, direct feedstock savings scale 
non-linearly. Your models must apply a 1.5x to 2.0x multiplier to the raw 
feedstock delta: every $\$10/mt$ saved on the Pro-Nap spread translates to an 
actual $+ \$15$ to $\$20/mt$ expansion in net cracker margins [2, 4]. 

### II. The Net Cracking Margin (NCM) Yield Matrix
A true quantitative framework must optimize across the entire product yield 
slate, calculating the Net Cracking Margin ($NCM$) as a vector of co-products. 
LPG cracking yields a high ratio of propylene to ethylene, whereas naphtha 
yields heavy aromatics (Benzene, Toluene, Xylene - BTX) and butadiene [2, 5]. 

Program Endur’s Advanced Portfolio Management (APM) to continuously calculate 
the margin delta ($\Delta NCM$) between the two feedstocks:

$$NCM_{Feed} = \sum_{i} \left( Y_{i}^{Feed} \times P_i \right) - C_{Feed} - 
OpEx_{Feed}$$

Where:
*   $i \in \{Ethylene (C_2), Propylene (C_3), Butadiene (C_4), Aromatics 
(BTX)\}$
*   $Y_{i}^{Feed}$: The specific volumetric yield ratio for product $i$ given 
the feedstock. 
*   $P_i$: Real-time CFR Northeast Asia spot price for the co-product.
*   $C_{Feed}$: Delivered cost of the feedstock (AFEI for Propane vs. CFR Japan
Naphtha).

The instantaneous switching trigger is defined as:
$$\Delta NCM = NCM_{LPG} - NCM_{Naphtha} > \Phi_{Friction}$$

*Note:* $\Phi_{Friction}$ represents the operational friction cost. Switching 
feedstocks is not instantaneous; it requires 24 to 72 hours of thermal and 
recovery system adjustments during which plant efficiency degrades [2, 6]. Your
model must amortize this efficiency loss across the planned run-time of the new
feedstock to prevent false-positive switching signals in algorithmic execution.

### III. Co-Product Valuation and the "Butadiene Trap"
Traders relying solely on the $\$50/mt$ Pro-Nap spread often get burned by the 
"Butadiene Trap." Because switching from naphtha to LPG drastically slashes the
yield of butadiene and aromatics [6, 7], the absolute prices of these heavier 
co-products can veto a seemingly profitable Pro-Nap spread. 

If Asian downstream demand for synthetic rubber spikes and $P_{Butadiene}$ 
explodes, the $NCM_{Naphtha}$ will remain superior due to the heavy 
$Y_{C_4}^{Naphtha}$ weighting, even if propane is trading at a massive 
$\$60/mt$ to $\$80/mt$ discount [7]. Hardcode an alert into your trading 
architecture: if the blended cracker margin remains negative due to collapsing 
ethylene/propylene prices, or if butadiene scarcity premiums skyrocket, 
override the standard Pro-Nap buy signal, as crackers will refuse to increase 
LPG run rates [7, 8].
