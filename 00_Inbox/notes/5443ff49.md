### 1) Core Market Logic (Benchmark Microstructure & Liquidity)
The AFEI is the dominant spot benchmark for LPG delivered into Asia. It is 
calculated daily as an exact 50/50 average of the CFR Japan (fixed price) and 
CFR South China (fixed price) assessments [1, 2]. Argus publishes this at 5:00 
pm Singapore time [3]. 

However, the physical reality is that the spot LPG market suffers from a severe
lack of liquidity. Often, only 1 to 2 actual fixed-price physical deals occur 
in a given week [4, 5]. Because physical deals are so rare, Argus reporters 
must survey 20 to 30 market participants daily and heavily interpolate the 
index using bids, offers, and—crucially—financial swap values [5-7]. 

**Liquidity Impact:** This lack of physical liquidity means the AFEI is highly 
sensitive to trader sentiment [8]. More importantly, because Argus accepts 
relatively small deals (11,000 tonnes or more) that do not strictly need to 
meet high-end vessel acceptability standards [9], the physical benchmark is 
vulnerable to being influenced by aggressive trading houses executing small 
physical cargoes at extreme prices to benefit their massive paper swap books. 

### 2) Key Concepts (Assessment Window Risks)
*   **The 25-40 Day Forward Window:** The AFEI does not assess "prompt" 
delivery. It evaluates the value of cargoes arriving 25 to 40 days forward from
the date of publication [10, 11]. 
*   **Half-Month Pricing Increments:** Within that 25-40 day window, physical 
prices are evaluated and discussed in terms of the First Half (H1) and Second 
Half (H2) of the month. The daily AFEI assessment is a proportionate average of
the days falling into these specific half-month periods [12, 13].
*   **Crossover Cargoes:** Cargoes scheduled to load or discharge exactly at 
the end of one month or the start of the next. To prevent buyers from 
deliberately manipulating the physical schedule to optimize against paper 
market gains, these are assessed strictly on a "flat price" basis [14]. 
*   **Part-Parcels:** Argus recognizes deals of 11,000 tonnes (roughly a 
quarter of a standard VLGC), which allows for cheaper freight and lower-spec 
vessels to set the price for the entire region [9].

### 3) How Traders Actually Use This (Paper vs Physical Interaction)
There is a massive feedback loop between the physical AFEI and the financial 
swaps market. Standard AFEI propane swaps trade in minimum lots of 2,000 tonnes
and settle against the monthly average of the physical AFEI index [15]. 

Because physical spot deals are so rare, Argus explicitly uses the derivatives 
(swaps) market to evaluate the outright value of floating-price physical deals 
[16, 17]. Advanced traders use this circularity to their advantage. A trader 
holding a massive "Long" paper swap position might deliberately bid up a small 
physical part-parcel in the Argus MOC (Market on Close) window. By taking a 
small loss on the physical leg to push the published AFEI higher, they engineer
a massive payout on their financially settled swaps.

### 4) Step-by-Step Trading Example (Benchmark Arbitrage)
A core strategy is arbitraging the structural timing differences between the 
AFEI and the Saudi Contract Price (CP). 
*   **The Setup:** The AFEI is a highly responsive, leading spot indicator, 
while the Saudi CP is a lagging indicator that trails the AFEI by approximately
21 days [18, 19]. 
*   **The Event:** A flood of cheap US cargoes hits Asia, crashing the AFEI 
spot price by $60/mt *(Illustrative)* over two weeks. 
*   **The Arb Trade:** Knowing the 21-day lag rule, a trader knows Saudi Aramco
will be forced to aggressively lower next month's CP to reflect this new 
reality [18, 19]. The trader immediately aggressively shorts front-month CP 
Swaps.
*   **The Execution:** Term buyers who lift on a CP basis are stuck paying 
artificially high prices for 3 weeks during the falling market [19]. But our 
trader, having front-run the lag, buys back the CP swaps after Aramco announces
the heavily discounted official price, capturing the arbitrage spread.

### 5) Trader Decision Process
**Go:**
*   You execute a physical deal where the delivery window (e.g., H1 of the 
month) matches a highly liquid, specific financial instrument (like a financial
"physical" that settles on specific days rather than a monthly average).
*   The East-West spread (AFEI swap minus CIF ARA swap) widens enough to 
confidently pull US cargoes to Asia instead of Europe [20].

**No-Go (Trade Killers):**
*   **Crossover Exposure:** Agreeing to a floating price on a crossover cargo 
without strictly defining the pricing days, leaving the desk exposed to the 
counterparty dragging their feet on discharge to catch a cheaper pricing month 
[14].
*   **Naked Quality Basis:** Pricing a premium Brunei field-grade cargo against
an AFEI index that has been artificially depressed by low-quality, 11,000-tonne
part-parcels moving on substandard vessels [9].

### 6) Common Trader Mistakes
*   **Assuming the Benchmark is "The Market":** Believing the daily AFEI print 
represents deep, systemic consensus. Because it is often based on just 1 or 2 
deals a week, it frequently reflects the specific logistical or paper-hedging 
agenda of the two counterparties involved in that specific trade [4, 5].
*   **Timing Mismatches:** Selling a physical cargo that prices during a tight 
3-day laycan, but hedging it with a standard AFEI swap that settles on the 
average of all 30 days in the month. If the market crashes *after* your ship 
discharges, your physical revenue is locked high, but your swap payout drops, 
destroying the hedge.
