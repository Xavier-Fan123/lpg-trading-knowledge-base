### 1) Core Market Logic
Freight is the most volatile variable in the LPG arbitrage, frequently 
representing 20-30% of the total delivered cost of a cargo [3]. The global LPG 
trade relies almost entirely on VLGCs to move massive volumes across oceans 
efficiently. 

Unlike standard crude oil tankers, VLGCs require highly specialized, expensive 
cryogenic steel tanks to keep the LPG refrigerated in liquid form [4, 5]. 
Because these vessels are expensive to build and operate, the global fleet is 
relatively small and inflexible in the short term (currently around 421 ships) 
[6]. Therefore, any event that forces ships to take longer routes—like a 
drought in the Panama Canal or conflict in the Red Sea—keeps vessels on the 
water longer. This artificially shrinks the available supply of ships, causing 
spot freight charter rates to skyrocket and altering the fundamental economics 
of global arbitrage [6, 7].

### 2) Key Concepts
*   **VLGC Capacity:** Very Large Gas Carriers typically transport 44,000 to 
47,000 metric tonnes of refrigerated LPG (roughly 75,000 to 85,000 cubic 
meters), usually utilizing four separate tanks that can handle both propane and
butane [4, 6].
*   **Tonne-Mile Demand Inflation:** When ships are forced to avoid canals and 
take longer routes (like the Cape of Good Hope), the total distance the global 
fleet must travel increases. This "tonne-mile inflation" absorbs vessel 
capacity and drives up spot charter rates [6, 7].
*   **Panama Canal Constraints:** A critical chokepoint for US-to-Asia flows. 
When water levels in Gatun Lake drop below 26 meters due to drought, the canal 
authority triggers freshwater surcharges, draft reductions, and caps daily 
transits, forcing shippers into expensive transit slot auctions [8-10].
*   **Cape of Good Hope (COGH) Routing:** The alternative to the Panama or Suez
canals. Routing via COGH adds roughly 10 to 15 days to a US-Asia voyage, 
drastically increasing time, fuel costs, and P&L exposure [6].
*   **War Risk & Insurance Constraints:** If Protection and Indemnity (P&I) 
clubs cancel war risk insurance for chokepoints like the Strait of Hormuz 
(where 95% of Middle East LPG transits), commercial fleets will completely 
abandon the route, stranding cargoes and sparking massive supply crises [11, 
12].
*   **Forward Freight Agreements (FFAs):** Financial derivative instruments 
(futures contracts) used to hedge freight rate risk on specific routes (e.g., 
Houston-to-Chiba or Middle East-to-Japan) [3, 13]. 

### 3) How Traders Actually Use This
Professional traders treat freight not as a static cost, but as an actively 
traded spread. 

When looking at the US-to-Asia arbitrage, a trader constantly calculates the 
Netback: $P_{Destination} - Freight - Canal\ Costs - Insurance - Terminal\ 
Fees$ [14]. If the AFEI price in Asia is high enough to cover Mont Belvieu 
prices plus all these logistics costs, the arb is open [14]. 

To protect this margin, traders actively hedge the freight leg using FFAs. If a
trader commits to moving a physical cargo from Houston to Chiba in three 
months, they will buy an FFA for that route today [3]. If Panama Canal delays 
cause spot VLGC rates to spike from $40,000/day to $80,000/day before the cargo
loads, the physical shipping cost doubles, but the financial FFA payout 
perfectly offsets the loss, protecting the trader's arbitrage margin [3].

### 4) Step-by-Step Trading Example
Let’s look at how a trader utilizes FFAs to protect an arbitrage margin amidst 
canal volatility. 
*   **The Setup:** A trader buys 44,000 mt of US LPG FOB loading in two months,
aiming to sell it CFR North Asia. The gross price spread (AFEI minus Mont 
Belvieu) is $150/mt *(Illustrative)*.
*   **The Freight Hedge:** Current spot VLGC freight for Houston-to-Chiba is 
$100/mt *(Illustrative)*. The trader buys 44,000 mt of Houston-to-Chiba FFAs at
$100/mt to lock in the logistics cost [3]. 
*   **The Shock:** A severe drought hits Gatun Lake. The Panama Canal Authority
slashes daily transit slots [10]. Spot VLGC freight rates explode to $140/mt 
due to vessels being forced to route around the Cape of Good Hope [6].
*   **The P&L Impact:** 
    *   *Physical Leg:* The trader must pay the shipowner the new $140/mt rate,
bleeding $40/mt from the expected physical margin.
    *   *Paper Leg:* The FFA settles against the new higher index. The trader 
earns a $40/mt payout on the FFA.
    *   *Result:* The freight spike is completely neutralized. The trader 
preserves their original $50/mt net profit ($150 gross spread - $100 hedged 
freight).

### 5) Trader Decision Process
**Go:**
*   The spread between origin and destination easily covers the FFA-hedged 
freight rate, terminal fees, and canal tolls [3, 14].
*   Vessel laycan (loading window) perfectly matches the terminal's available 
loading slots, avoiding demurrage. 

**No-Go (Trade Killers):**
*   **Canal Auction Blowouts:** The cost to win a Panama Canal transit slot 
auction wipes out the entire arbitrage margin [9].
*   **Insurance Voidance:** War risk insurance is suddenly pulled for the Gulf 
of Oman, meaning your chartered vessel legally cannot enter the Middle East to 
lift your FOB cargo [12].
*   **Stranded Unhedged Freight:** You buy physical LPG but wait to charter a 
ship, leaving your freight floating. If rates spike due to a geopolitical 
event, your physical cargo is "under water" before it even loads.

### 6) Common Trader Mistakes
*   **Static Netback Models:** Assuming transit times and canal fees are fixed 
numbers. A 5-day delay waiting for a canal slot incurs demurrage (often 
$50,000+ per day) and entirely alters the delivered cost of the cargo [15].
*   **Ignoring Tonne-Mile Ripples:** Failing to realize that a disruption in 
the Red Sea impacts US-to-Asia rates. When ships route around Africa, the 
*global* fleet gets stretched thin, lifting charter rates universally, not just
in the disrupted region [7, 16].
*   **Focusing on Flat Price, Forgetting Freight:** Executing perfect hedges on
the LPG molecule (Mont Belvieu vs AFEI) but leaving the freight completely 
unhedged, turning a safe arbitrage trade into a massive speculative bet on the 
shipping market [3, 17].

### 7) Desk Exercise
You are pricing a 44,000 mt propane cargo from the US Gulf Coast to Chiba, 
Japan.
*   Current AFEI - USGC spread: $140/mt *(Illustrative)*.
*   Base Freight via Panama: $90/mt *(Illustrative)*. Terminal/Port Fees: 
$10/mt.
*   **The Event:** You receive a morning alert: Gatun Lake has dropped below 26
meters. The canal authority triggers freshwater surcharges and restricts slots 
[10]. 
*   **The Dilemma:** You have two choices for your unhedged vessel:
    *   *Option A:* Wait in the queue for 6 days. Your daily demurrage/waiting 
cost is $50,000/day *(Illustrative)*.
    *   *Option B:* Buy an expedited auction slot for $440,000 *(Illustrative)*
to transit immediately. 

**Task:** Calculate the revised total freight + fee cost per tonne for *both* 
Option A and Option B. Which option provides the better netback? Does the 
$140/mt spread still cover the total costs for your chosen option?

### 8) Answer Framework
1.  **Calculate Option A (Wait):** Multiply the 6 days of delay by the $50,000 
daily demurrage rate. Divide that total penalty by 44,000 mt to find the 
per-tonne delay cost. Add this to the $90 base freight and $10 fees.
2.  **Calculate Option B (Auction):** Divide the $440,000 auction fee by 44,000
mt to find the per-tonne auction cost. Add this to the $90 base freight and $10
fees.
3.  **Compare & Decide:** Identify the lower-cost routing option.
4.  **Check Arb Viability:** Subtract your best total cost from the $140/mt 
gross spread to see if the trade remains profitable. 

### 9) Real-World Signals to Monitor
*   **Panama Canal Authority (ACP) Advisories:** Monitoring Gatun Lake levels; 
drops below 26m trigger surcharges and transit caps [10].
*   **Baltic / Argus FFA Rates:** Daily monitoring of Forward Freight 
Agreements on key routes (Houston-Chiba, Ras Tanura-Chiba) to gauge forward 
shipping sentiment [3, 18].
*   **P&I Club Insurance Alerts:** Watching for re-insurers canceling or 
spiking war risk coverage in critical chokepoints like the Strait of Hormuz or 
Red Sea [12, 19].
*   **Vessel Tracking Analytics:** Using tools like Kpler or Vortexa to track 
the ratio of laden vs. ballast ships and monitor how many VLGCs are diverting 
via the Cape of Good Hope [20].
