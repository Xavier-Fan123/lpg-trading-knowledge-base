Title: The Argus Far East Index: LPG Trading and Risk Management

Here is Episode 3 of your training program. In Episode 2, we covered the Saudi 
CP, which anchors your FOB supply costs. Today, we focus on the revenue side of
your P&L: the Argus Far East Index (AFEI). 

Coming from a risk management background, you are familiar with basis and 
timing risks. The AFEI is where those risks become highly concentrated because 
you are matching a daily, forward-looking spot assessment against a monthly 
average derivative settlement.

### 1) Core Market Logic
In the Asian market, the Argus Far East Index (AFEI) is the ultimate price 
setter for delivered spot cargoes. While the Saudi CP tells you what it costs 
to lift a cargo from the Middle East, the AFEI dictates the CFR (Cost and 
Freight) destination value for cargoes landing in North Asia and China ("The 
Professional LPG Trader’s Knowledge Handbook", Sec: Far East Index (FEI / 
AFEI)). For a trader, P&L is generated by trading the spread between these two 
indices. If the AFEI delivered price rises faster than your CP-based FOB costs 
plus freight, your arbitrage margin expands. Conversely, if you sell physical 
cargoes priced off the daily AFEI but hedge them improperly using monthly 
swaps, a sudden market structure shift (contango/backwardation) will bleed your
P&L through basis risk.

### 2) Key Concepts
*   **What AFEI Represents:** The AFEI is the average of the CFR Japan (fixed 
price) and CFR South China (fixed price) spot assessments, weighted 50/50 
("Review of the appropriateness of the current LPG international benchmark", 
Box 4.1). 
*   **The Delivery Window:** The AFEI does not price propane or butane 
delivered *today*. It is a forward-looking assessment for cargoes arriving 
25-40 days forward from the date of publication ("Argus International LPG 
methodology", Sec: Asia-Pacific). *(Note: An older source notes it as 20-35 
days forward ["Review of the appropriateness...", Box 4.1], but the current 
Argus methodology explicitly states 25-40 days).*
*   **The "Roll" Date:** The quotes for the AFEI roll forward to the next month
on the first working day after the 15th of each month ("Argus International LPG
methodology", Sec: International swaps prices; "Review of the 
appropriateness...", Box 4.1). 
*   **The Butane Specifics:** Because your desk trades butane, you must track 
the specific Argus Far East Index (butane) assessment, which utilizes the same 
25-40 days forward window but is assessed specifically for butane 
specifications ("Argus International LPG methodology", Sec: Asia-Pacific).
*   **Financial Swaps:** AFEI swaps are OTC financial instruments used to hedge
physical exposure. Unlike a physical deal that prices on a specific day, a swap
settles against the *monthly average* of the daily AFEI prices for a given 
contract month ("Use swaps, pre-buys and physicals to manage risk - LP Gas").
*   **The CP-to-AFEI Spread:** This spread tracks the relative strength of 
Mideast term supply versus Asian spot demand. A high CP premium to AFEI signals
that term supply is structurally expensive compared to spot alternatives ("The 
Professional LPG Trader’s Knowledge Handbook", Sec: Far East Index).

### 3) How Traders Actually Use This
On the desk, you will constantly monitor the CP-to-AFEI spread to decide 
whether your desk should lift your term Middle Eastern cargoes or reject/reduce
them in favor of buying spot CFR China cargoes. 

If you buy a physical cargo from the US or Middle East, it will take roughly 24
to 30 days to arrive in Asia. Because the physical price will fluctuate while 
the cargo is on the water, you must immediately sell an AFEI swap for the 
specific month your cargo will arrive. This "locks in" your CFR revenue. 
However, you must carefully align the arrival date of your vessel with the swap
month, because if your ship is delayed and arrives in a different month, you 
are exposed to extreme timing risk. Traders also trade the "Basis" purely on 
paper—buying CP swaps and selling AFEI swaps—as a pure financial bet on the 
arbitrage window opening or closing without ever touching a physical ship ("The
Professional LPG Trader’s Knowledge Handbook", Sec: The Derivatives Market).

### 4) Step-by-Step Trading Example
Let’s construct a paper basis trade capturing the CP-to-AFEI spread, known as 
trading the "East-West" or "Arb" on paper.
*(Note: All prices are illustrative)*

1.  **Identify the Spread:** You notice that prompt Saudi CP swaps are trading 
at $550/mt, while AFEI swaps for the same month are trading at $600/mt.
2.  **Evaluate Freight:** You check VLGC spot freight from Ras Tanura to 
Japan/South China. Freight is currently $40/mt ("Review of the 
appropriateness...", Sec 6.4).
3.  **Calculate Margin:** $600 (AFEI Swap) - $550 (CP Swap) - $40 (Freight) = 
**+$10/mt**. 
4.  **Execution (The Hedge):** Because you believe the AFEI will remain strong 
due to Chinese demand, you go long the spread. You buy 10,000 mt of the CP Swap
at $550/mt and simultaneously sell 10,000 mt of the AFEI swap at $600/mt.
5.  **Settlement:** At the end of the month, the average physical CP settles at
$560/mt, and the average physical AFEI settles at $620/mt. 
6.  **P&L:** You lose $10/mt on your CP leg (you bought at $550, it settled at 
$560 - *Correction: As a buyer of a swap, if the settlement is higher than your
strike, you gain the difference* ("Use swaps, pre-buys and physicals to manage 
risk - LP Gas")). So you gain $10/mt on the CP swap, and you lose $20/mt on the
AFEI swap (you sold at $600, it settled at $620). Wait, this means the spread 
widened to $60/mt ($620-$560). Since you locked it in at a $50/mt spread 
($600-$550), you *lost* money on the paper spread widening against your short 
AFEI position. 

### 5) Trader Decision Process
*   **Go:** You buy physical CFR South China butane on a spot basis explicitly 
indexed to the AFEI, and immediately sell the corresponding AFEI swap to lock 
in your trading margin.
*   **No-Go / Kill the Trade:** A delay in your vessel's ETA pushes your cargo 
from arriving on October 29th to November 2nd. Your short paper hedge is in 
October AFEI swaps, but your physical cargo will price in November. If the 
market is in steep backwardation (October prices are much higher than November 
prices), you will lose massive amounts of money when you roll your hedge 
forward.

### 6) Common Trader Mistakes
*   **Mismatching the 15th-of-the-Month Roll:** A common error is forgetting 
that the AFEI forward window rolls on the 16th. If you are quoting a physical 
deal on the 17th of January, the prompt AFEI index is no longer pricing 
February arrivals; it is now pricing March arrivals ("Argus International LPG 
methodology", Sec: International swaps prices). 
*   **Ignoring the Monthly Average Settlement:** Swaps settle against the 
*monthly average* of the index. If you deliver physical butane to a Chinese 
buyer during the first week of the month and close out your physical position, 
but your swap doesn't settle until the end of the month, a sudden crash in 
prices during the third week will destroy your margin ("Use swaps, pre-buys and
physicals to manage risk - LP Gas").

### 8) Answer Framework
*   **Step 1:** Match the vessel's arrival window (March 5th) to the 
corresponding swap month (March AFEI).
*   **Step 2:** Acknowledge how a swap settles (it averages the *entire* month 
of March). 
*   **Step 3:** Evaluate the backwardation risk. (If delivery is delayed to 
late March, how does that impact your physical pricing vs. your paper 
settlement?)

### 9) Real-World Signals to Monitor
*   **The 16th of the Month:** Watch the AFEI pricing window roll forward.
*   **AFEI Propane vs. AFEI Butane Spread:** As a butane trader, if the AFEI 
butane price drops $50/mt below AFEI propane, expect flexible crackers in Asia 
to immediately switch to cracking butane, supporting your physical position.
*   **East-West Spread (AFEI minus CIF ARA):** Indicates whether cargoes from 
the US/Mideast should sail to Asia or be diverted to Europe ("Argus 
International LPG methodology", Sec: Spreads).
