### 1) Core Market Logic
LPG is unique because it must be transported in a liquid state, which reduces 
its volume by a factor of 230 (for butane) to 270 (for propane) compared to its
gaseous state [1, 2]. Achieving this requires either extreme pressure or 
extreme refrigeration. 

Because of these physical properties, LPG infrastructure (both ships and 
storage tanks) is vastly more expensive to build than standard crude oil or 
petroleum product infrastructure [3]. Terminals are built to "just meet" parcel
sizes, leaving zero room for scheduling errors [4]. Operational failures—like a
vessel arriving too warm, or a terminal loading too slowly—trigger cascading 
delays that result in massive demurrage penalties, directly destroying the 
trader's calculated arbitrage margin [5, 6].

### 2) Key Concepts
*   **Boil-Off and Reliquefaction:** Fully refrigerated Very Large Gas Carriers
(VLGCs) carry LPG at atmospheric pressure and extreme cold (e.g., -42°C to 
-50°C) [7]. During long voyages, heat ingress causes the cargo to naturally 
evaporate or "boil-off." To maintain the liquid state and prevent pressure 
buildup, VLGCs use onboard reliquefaction plants that constantly compress, 
cool, and return the boil-off gas to the tanks [5, 7]. 
*   **Boil-Off Commercial Sharing:** **Not found in sources.** *(The sources

standard LPG Sales and Purchase Agreement (SPA) templates to our knowledge

*   **Tank Types (Refrigerated vs. Pressurized):** 
    *   *Fully Refrigerated VLGCs (>70,000 cbm):* Use heavily insulated 
prismatic "Type A" tanks at low pressure (0.28 kg/cm²) [7].
    *   *Fully Pressurized Ships (<15,000 cbm):* Use heavy steel cylindrical or
spherical "Type C" tanks that carry cargo at ambient temperature and high 
pressure (up to 17.5 kg/cm²). They do not require insulation or reliquefaction 
[7]. 
*   **Terminal Handling Restrictions (Pre-Cooling):** Before a refrigerated 
cargo can be unloaded into or loaded from a terminal, the transfer pipelines 
must be "pre-cooled." A small stream of refrigerated product is run through the
lines to lower them from ambient temperature to the product's temperature. This
prevents rapid vaporization, which could cause "equipment freeze" and severe 
cryogenic blockages [2, 9].
*   **Tank Prep & Hydrate Blockages:** Before loading, tanks must be dried to a
dew point of < -40°C, inerted to < 5% oxygen, and cooled down at a strict rate 
of < 10°C per hour. If the tanks aren't properly dried, trace water vapor 
freezes during the cool-down, creating ice-like "hydrates" that block pumps and
valves, delaying operations by days [5, 10].
*   **Demurrage and Laytime Rules:** Laytime (allowed loading/discharging time)
for LPG is incredibly strict. Under standard Shell terms, laytime is 24 hours 
for an LPG delivery, 24 hours for vessels under 15,000 tons, and 36 hours for 
all other cases [11]. If discharge takes longer, demurrage is charged, 
typically at daily rates between $18,500 and $22,000 for smaller tankers or up 
to $50,000+ for VLGCs *(Illustrative based on source ranges)* [5, 6].

### 3) How Traders Actually Use This
Traders use these operational nuances to allocate risk during contract 
negotiations. 

When exporting our Hengyi Brunei production to China, we must match the ship 
type to the buyer's terminal. If a Chinese buyer only has pressurized storage 
spheres, we cannot charter a fully-refrigerated VLGC to deliver the cargo, as 
the buyer's terminal lacks the heating facilities to bring the -42°C liquid up 
to ambient temperature for pressurized storage [12]. We must charter a 
pressurized coaster or a semi-refrigerated vessel instead [7].

To protect our P&L from "volume slippage" (the difference between loaded and 
discharged volume due to temperature expansion/contraction or boil-off), we 
strongly prefer to sell our CFR cargoes based on **"Load Port Quantity."** This
means the buyer pays based on the Bill of Lading issued in Brunei. If we agree 
to "Outturn Quantity" (Discharge Port settlement), we absorb the financial loss
of any molecule that boiled off or slipped during the voyage to China [5].

### 4) Step-by-Step Trading Example
Let’s look at a demurrage and pre-cooling dispute on a CFR delivery.
*   **The Setup:** You sell 44,000 mt of refrigerated propane CFR South China. 
Your chartered VLGC arrives on time. Laytime allowed is 36 hours [11]. 
*   **The Event:** The ship tenders its Notice of Readiness (NOR). However, the
receiving terminal failed to adequately pre-cool their loading arms and 
transfer pipelines [9]. 
*   **The Consequence:** When discharge begins, the -42°C propane hits the warm
shore pipes, instantly vaporizing and causing a pressure spike. Operations must
be halted for 48 hours to safely resolve the equipment freeze and properly cool
the lines [2]. 
*   **The P&L Impact:** The total discharge takes 84 hours (48 hours over the 
36-hour laytime). At a demurrage rate of $50,000/day *(Illustrative)*, the 
shipowner bills you $100,000. 
*   **The Resolution:** Because the delay was caused by the shore-side 
equipment failure (lack of pre-cooling), the Statement of Facts (SOF) signed by
the Master will log this against the terminal [6]. As the CFR seller, you pass 
this $100,000 demurrage claim directly to your Chinese buyer, protecting your 
trading margin.

### 5) Trader Decision Process
**Go:**
*   Quantity settlement is strictly tied to Load Port inspection (e.g., SGS 
certificate at Brunei).
*   The nominated vessel's temperature/pressure capabilities perfectly match 
the receiving terminal's storage limits.
*   Charterparty terms clearly define who pays for weather delays (e.g., 
half-rate demurrage for bad weather).

**No-Go (Trade Killers):**
*   **Mismatched Infrastructure:** Attempting to discharge a fully refrigerated
VLGC into a small pressurized terminal without onboard cargo heaters [13]. 
*   **Hydrate Risks:** The independent inspector notes the vessel's tanks have 
a dew point warmer than -40°C before loading. You must halt loading, or you 
risk destroying the vessel's pumps with ice blockages [5].
*   **Open-Ended Laytime:** A buyer demands laytime commences only *after* the 
ship berths, rather than upon tendering NOR at the anchorage, leaving you 
exposed to 10 days of unpaid waiting if the port is congested.

### 6) Common Trader Mistakes
*   **Ignoring Energy Density vs. Mass:** Hedging financial swaps in metric 
tonnes, but failing to realize the physical volume loaded onto the ship 
fluctuated due to loading temperatures. This leaves the trader with unmatched 
paper volume (basis risk) [5, 8].
*   **Underestimating Terminal Constraints:** Booking a 44,000 mt VLGC to a 
terminal that only has 25,000 mt of available ullage (empty tank space). The 
ship is forced to wait (incurring demurrage) or sail partially loaded 
(incurring deadfreight). 
*   **Sloppy SOF Review:** Failing to train operations staff to rigorously 
check the Statement of Facts (SOF). If your Ops team accepts an SOF that blames
a 2-day delay on "ship's reliquefaction failure" instead of "shore tank lack of
ullage," you cannot pass the demurrage bill to the buyer [6].

### 9) Real-World Signals to Monitor
*   **Statement of Facts (SOF):** Reviewed daily during active discharge to 
track every minute of delay and assign fault (ship vs. shore vs. weather) [6].

discrepancies between a specific ship's measured volume and actual shore tank
