## Overview

The Saudi Aramco Contract Price (CP) is the **monthly benchmark** for propane and butane exported from the Middle East under term contracts. Published retroactively by [[Saudi Aramco]] around the end of each month (or early in the following month), the CP sets the price for cargoes that have **already been loaded** during that month. It is the single most influential administered price in the global LPG market.

## Retroactive Pricing Mechanism

Unlike most commodity benchmarks that are forward-looking, the CP operates **retroactively**:

1. Cargoes are nominated and loaded throughout month M
2. Saudi Aramco announces the CP for month M at the end of M or early M+1
3. Buyers pay based on the announced CP, regardless of where spot markets traded during loading

This creates a unique dynamic where **buyers bear price risk between loading and price announcement**. The CP is influenced by but not mechanically linked to the [[AFEI Benchmark]] or other spot references. Aramco considers multiple factors including:

- Far East spot market levels during the month
- Northwest European market conditions
- Supply-demand balance assessments
- Strategic market share considerations

## CP vs Spot Market Dynamics

The relationship between CP and spot markets creates tradeable spreads:

- **CP premium**: When CP exceeds the average of AFEI spot during the month, term buyers are disadvantaged
- **CP discount**: When CP is below spot averages, term contract holders benefit
- Traders with both term and spot positions can exploit this by **optimizing cargo allocation** between term and spot channels

## Term Contracts vs Spot Shift

Historically, the majority of Middle East LPG moved under multi-year term contracts priced at CP. A structural shift has occurred:

- Term contract volumes have declined as buyers seek pricing flexibility
- Spot and short-term deal share has grown, particularly from non-Saudi Middle East producers (Qatar, UAE, Kuwait)
- The rise of US exports priced off [[Mont Belvieu]] has provided an alternative origin, reducing CP's monopoly pricing power
- [[Chinese PDH Margin|Chinese PDH operators]] increasingly blend term CP cargoes with spot purchases to optimize feedstock cost

## Empirical Facts vs Analytical Assumptions

### Empirical Facts
- Saudi Aramco announces CP retroactively, typically at the end of the loading month or early the following month (Aramco official announcements)
- Propane CP traded in the range of $400-700/mt during 2023-2025 (Aramco historical CP data)
- Butane CP traded in the range of $380-680/mt during 2023-2025 (Aramco historical CP data)
- Typical CP-to-AFEI spread is +/- $10-30/mt (market data, broker reports)
- Term contract volumes from the Middle East have declined as a share of total trade (Argus, Poten & Partners)
- US LPG exports have grown to provide an alternative origin to Middle East CP-priced cargoes (EIA export data)

### Analytical Assumptions
- The characterization of CP as the "single most influential administered price" is a market consensus view; its influence is gradually declining relative to spot benchmarks
- The assumption that Aramco considers "strategic market share considerations" in CP setting is widely believed but not officially confirmed by Aramco
- The structural shift toward spot pricing is assumed to continue; Aramco could reverse this by offering more competitive term structures
- The claim that CP is "not mechanically linked" to AFEI is an inference from observed pricing patterns; Aramco's internal methodology is not publicly disclosed

## Scenario Analysis

### Base Case
- CP continues to be set within $10-30/mt of AFEI monthly averages, maintaining its role as the Middle East term pricing anchor
- Term contract share stabilizes as buyers value supply security alongside pricing flexibility
- CP influence gradually diminishes as Asian spot market liquidity deepens

### Bull Case
- Middle East supply tightens (OPEC+ decisions, maintenance, geopolitical disruption), restoring CP pricing power
- Aramco sets CP at significant premiums to AFEI, reflecting supply scarcity
- Term contract holders benefit from supply security during tight markets, reversing the spot shift trend

### Bear Case
- Sustained US export growth provides abundant alternative supply, marginalizing CP pricing power
- Aramco sets CP at persistent discounts to retain market share, compressing margins for CP-formula traders
- Non-Saudi Middle East producers (Qatar, UAE, Kuwait) shift entirely to spot pricing, isolating Saudi CP
- CP announcement timing uncertainty increases, creating wider basis risk windows

### Invalidation Triggers
- Saudi Aramco abandons the retroactive CP mechanism in favor of a formula-based or exchange-linked pricing system
- Middle East LPG export share drops below 20% of seaborne trade, reducing CP's market relevance
- Establishment of an Asian LPG exchange with sufficient liquidity to serve as the universal pricing reference

## Downside Risks

- **Retroactive pricing risk**: Buyers bear price risk between loading and CP announcement; adverse CP settings can erase anticipated margins -- see [[Basis Risk Management]] for managing CP announcement exposure
- **CP-AFEI spread blowout**: Divergence between CP and spot market levels during volatile months can create significant mark-to-market swings on term positions
- **Supply concentration risk**: Heavy reliance on CP-priced Middle East term cargoes concentrates geopolitical and counterparty risk -- see [[Demurrage and Laytime]] for Middle East port-specific operational risks
- **Hedging mismatch**: Paper hedges referenced to AFEI swaps against CP-priced physical cargoes create structural basis risk -- see [[Basis Risk Management]] for CP-vs-AFEI basis treatment
