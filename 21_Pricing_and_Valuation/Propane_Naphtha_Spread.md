## Core Mechanics

The **Propane-Naphtha (Pro-Nap) spread** measures the relative cost of propane versus naphtha as a petrochemical feedstock, primarily for steam crackers producing ethylene. It is one of the most closely watched indicators in LPG trading because it directly drives [[Feedstock Switching Economics|feedstock switching decisions]] at flexible steam crackers, particularly in Northeast Asia.

$$\text{Pro-Nap Spread} = P_{\text{propane}} - P_{\text{naphtha}} \quad (\text{USD/mt})$$

A negative spread (propane cheaper than naphtha) favors propane cracking; a positive spread favors naphtha.

## Critical Thresholds and Decision Rules

Two empirical rules govern switching behavior:

1. **Absolute spread trigger**: When propane trades at a discount of **$50/mt or more** below naphtha, steam crackers begin shifting feedstock slate toward propane
2. **Price ratio rule**: When the propane/naphtha ratio falls below **90%** (i.e., propane is less than 90% of the naphtha price), switching economics become compelling even if the absolute spread is narrower

These thresholds are not binary switches but rather **probability-weighted inflection points** where incremental cracker capacity starts to move.

## Operational Leverage Multiplier

Switching from naphtha to propane creates an **operational leverage multiplier of 1.5-2.0x** on the cracker's margin:

- Propane yields more ethylene per tonne (~42-45% ethylene yield vs ~30-32% for naphtha)
- However, propane yields **zero aromatics** (benzene, toluene, xylene) and significantly less butadiene
- The net cracking margin (NCM) must account for co-product losses

## Net Cracking Margin Formula

$$\text{NCM} = \sum_{i}(Y_i \times P_i) - C_{\text{feed}} - \text{OpEx}$$

Where:
- $Y_i$ = yield of co-product $i$ (ethylene, propylene, butadiene, aromatics, fuel oil)
- $P_i$ = market price of co-product $i$
- $C_{\text{feed}}$ = feedstock cost (propane or naphtha)
- OpEx = operating expenditure including utilities, catalysts, maintenance

## The Butadiene Trap

A critical risk in spread-driven switching: the **butadiene trap**. When multiple crackers simultaneously switch to propane:

- Butadiene supply collapses (propane cracking yields ~1-2% butadiene vs ~4-5% from naphtha)
- Butadiene prices spike, improving naphtha cracking margins
- This creates a **negative feedback loop** that narrows the Pro-Nap spread
- Traders who anticipated sustained propane demand from switching can be caught by the reversal

## Co-Product Valuation

| Product | Naphtha Yield | Propane Yield |
|---------|---------------|---------------|
| Ethylene | 30-32% | 42-45% |
| Propylene | 13-16% | 3-5% |
| Butadiene | 4-5% | 1-2% |
| Aromatics (BTX) | 15-20% | 0% |
| Fuel oil/gas | Balance | Balance |

The Pro-Nap spread is a critical input for [[Chinese PDH Margin]] analysis, as PDH competes with steam cracking for propane feedstock. See also [[AFEI Benchmark]] for propane pricing and [[Feedstock Switching Economics]] for the broader decision framework.

## Empirical Facts vs Analytical Assumptions

### Empirical Facts
- Propane ethylene yield is approximately 42-45%; naphtha ethylene yield is approximately 30-32% (industry engineering data, IHS/CMAI)
- Naphtha yields approximately 15-20% aromatics (BTX); propane yields zero aromatics (petrochemical engineering data)
- Butadiene yield from naphtha cracking is approximately 4-5% vs 1-2% from propane cracking (industry data)
- Co-product yields (propylene, butadiene, aromatics, fuel) are well-established from decades of operating data (IHS, Nexant)

### Analytical Assumptions
- The $50/mt absolute spread trigger for switching is an empirically observed threshold, not a fixed rule; actual switching points vary by cracker configuration, location, and co-product values
- The 90% price ratio rule is a heuristic; individual operators may use different thresholds depending on their co-product exposure and downstream integration
- The "operational leverage multiplier of 1.5-2.0x" is an approximation that varies with market conditions and co-product price levels
- The butadiene trap is a recognized risk pattern but its severity depends on the scale of simultaneous switching and butadiene inventory levels
- Thresholds are described as "probability-weighted inflection points," which acknowledges uncertainty but implies a distribution that may not hold in all market conditions

## Scenario Analysis

### Base Case
- Pro-Nap spread oscillates around -$20 to -$60/mt, with propane maintaining a moderate feedstock discount to naphtha
- Flexible crackers allocate 20-40% of feedstock slate to propane, providing steady incremental LPG demand
- Co-product prices remain balanced, preventing extreme switching in either direction

### Bull Case
- Crude oil price spike lifts naphtha above $700/mt while propane remains anchored by abundant US supply below $550/mt
- Pro-Nap spread widens beyond -$100/mt, triggering aggressive switching to propane
- Propane demand surges from cracker switching + PDH, tightening the LPG market and supporting AFEI prices
- China consumption tax alpha amplifies the switching incentive for Chinese crackers

### Bear Case
- Crude oil collapse brings naphtha below $400/mt, making it competitive with or cheaper than propane
- Pro-Nap spread turns positive, eliminating propane cracking demand
- Butadiene trap materializes: simultaneous switching collapses butadiene supply, spiking BD prices and reversing the spread
- Propane loses its feedstock premium, weakening the structural demand support

### Invalidation Triggers
- Emergence of on-purpose butadiene production technology eliminates the butadiene trap mechanism
- All new cracker capacity is built as dedicated ethane crackers, removing feedstock switching flexibility
- Naphtha permanently decouples from crude oil pricing (e.g., through oversupply from mega-refineries), breaking the historical Pro-Nap relationship

## Downside Risks

- **Butadiene trap reversal risk**: Aggressive positioning for sustained propane cracking demand can be unwound rapidly when the BD price spike reverses the spread -- see [[Basis Risk Management]] for managing co-product correlation risk
- **Switching lag and friction costs**: The 24-72 hour transition period means spread traders face execution risk during rapid market moves -- see [[Demurrage and Laytime]] for feedstock delivery timing mismatches
- **Co-product valuation uncertainty**: NCM calculations depend on multiple product prices; errors in co-product price forecasting create hidden exposure
- **Correlation breakdown**: The Pro-Nap spread assumes a stable relationship between oil-linked naphtha and gas-linked propane; divergence in oil and gas markets can produce extreme and persistent spread moves -- see [[Basis Risk Management]]
